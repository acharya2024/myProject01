Algebra

Algebra forms the foundation of physics, enabling us to express physical relationships concisely and solve problems using symbols and equations.

Variables and Constants:

Variables represent quantities that can change, such as position (\(x\)), time (\(t\)), speed (\(v\)), or temperature (\(T\)). Constants, in contrast, have fixed values, like acceleration due to gravity (\(g\)), speed of light (\(c\)), or the mass of an electron (\(m_e\)).

  • Example 1: Free Fall

    In the equation \(h = \frac{1}{2}gt^2\), \(h\) (height) and \(t\) (time) are variables, while \(g\) (acceleration due to gravity) is a constant.

  • Example 2: Ohm's Law

    In Ohm's law, \(V = IR\), voltage (\(V\)) and current (\(I\)) might be variables, while resistance (\(R\)) could be constant depending on the circuit.

Algebraic Expressions and Equations:

Algebraic expressions combine variables, constants, and mathematical operations. An equation states that two expressions are equal.

  • Example 1: Kinetic Energy

    The kinetic energy of an object is given by the expression \(\frac{1}{2}mv^2\), where \(m\) is mass and \(v\) is velocity.

  • Example 2: Newton's Second Law

    Newton's second law is represented by the equation \(F = ma\), where \(F\) is force, \(m\) is mass, and \(a\) is acceleration.

Working with Units:

Nearly all physical quantities have units, which tell us what scale or standard is used to measure the quantity. Using consistent units in equations is crucial for getting correct results. Inconsistent units can lead to incorrect calculations and misinterpretations of physical phenomena.

  • Example 1: Converting Area Units

    Suppose you have a square with sides of length 2 meters. Its area is \(2 \, \text{m} \times 2 \, \text{m} = 4 \, \text{m}^2\). To express this area in square centimeters (\(\text{cm}^2\)), we need to convert meters to centimeters.

    Since, 1 meter = 100 centimeters, \(2 \, \text{m} = 200 \, \text{cm}\).

    Thus the area can be written as \(200 \, \text{cm} \times 200 \, \text{cm} = 40000 \, \text{cm}^2\). Notice that \(4 \, \text{m}^2 = 4 \times (100 \, \text{cm})^2 = 4 \times 10000 \, \text{cm}^2 = 40000 \, \text{cm}^2\).

  • Example 2: Converting Speed Units

    A car is traveling at 72 kilometers per hour (km/h). To express this speed in meters per second (m/s), we use the conversions 1 km = 1000 m and 1 h = 3600 s:

    \( \qquad 72 \, \text{km/h} = \frac{72 \times 1000 \, \text{m}}{3600 \, \text{s}} = \frac{72000}{3600} \, \text{m/s} = 20 \, \text{m/s} \)

    Note that we multiplied by 1000 (to convert km to m) and divided by 3600 (to convert h to s).

  • Example 3: Unit Consistency in Equations

    In the equation \(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\), each term must have the same units (units of distance). If \(s\) is measured in meters (m), \(u\) (initial velocity) should be in meters per second (m/s), \(t\) (time) in seconds (s), and \(a\) (acceleration) in meters per second squared (m/sÂ²).

    Let's check: \(ut\) has units of \(\text{m/s} \cdot \text{s} = \text{m}\) and \(\frac{1}{2}at^2\) has units of \(\text{m/s}^2 \cdot \text{s}^2 = \text{m}\). All terms have units of meters, ensuring dimensional consistency.

  • Example 4: Another Example of Conversion

    Newton's law of gravitation states that \(F=G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\), where \(F\) is the force, \(G\) is gravitational constant, \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) are the masses of two objects and \(r\) is the distance between them.

    The value of G in SI units is \(6.674 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2/\text{kg}^2 \). If we consider all the masses in grams and distances in cm, then what will be the value of \(G\) in CGS units?

    Since, \(1 \, \text{N} = 10^5 \, \text{dyn}\), \(1 \, \text{m} = 100 \, \text{cm}\) and \(1 \, \text{kg} = 1000 \, \text{g}\) , thus \(G\) can be written as:

    \(\qquad G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \frac{\text{N m}^2}{\text{kg}^2} = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \frac{10^5 \text{dyn} \, (100 \, \text{cm})^2}{(1000 \, \text{g})^2} = 6.674 \times 10^{-8} \, \text{dyn cm}^2/\text{g}^2\)

Ratios and Proportions:

A ratio compares two quantities. A proportion equates two ratios. Direct proportion: One quantity increases as the other increases proportionally. Inverse proportion: One increases as the other decreases proportionally.

  • Example 1: Density

    Density is defined as the ratio of mass to volume: \(\rho = \frac{m}{V}\).

  • Example 2: Ideal Gas Law

    The ideal gas law, \(PV = nRT\), can be used to set up proportions. At constant temperature, pressure is inversely proportional to volume.

Exponents and Scientific Notation:

Exponents indicate repeated multiplication (e.g., \(10^3 = 10 \times 10 \times 10\)). Scientific notation expresses a number as a product of a number between 1 and 10 and a power of 10 (e.g., \(3 \times 10^8\)).

  • Example 1: Area of a Square

    If the side of a square is \(x\), its area is \(x^2\).

  • Example 2: Planck's Constant

    Planck's constant is approximately \(6.626 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{J s}\).

Quadratic equations, of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) (\(a \neq 0\)), are essential for solving various physics problems.

Factoring Methods:

Some quadratic equations can be solved by factoring the quadratic expression into two binomials.

  • Example 1: \(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\)

    Factoring gives \((x-2)(x-3)=0\), so the solutions are \(x=2\) and \(x=3\).

  • Example 2: \(2x^2 + 3x - 2 = 0\)

    Factoring gives \((2x - 1)(x + 2) = 0\), leading to solutions \(x=\frac{1}{2}\) and \(x=-2\).

Quadratic Formula:

The quadratic formula provides solutions for any quadratic equation: \(\qquad x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)

  • Example 1: \(x^2 + 2x - 3 = 0\)

    Applying the formula: \(x = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{2^2 - 4(1)(-3)}}{2(1)} = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{16}}{2} = \frac{-2 \pm 4}{2}\), giving \(x=1\) and \(x=-3\).

  • Example 2: \(3x^2 - 5x + 1 = 0\)

    Using the formula: \(x = \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{(-5)^2 - 4(3)(1)}}{2(3)} = \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{13}}{6}\).

Nature of Roots (Discriminant):

The discriminant, \(b^2 - 4ac\), determines the nature of the roots.

  • Example 1: \(x^2 - 4x + 4 = 0\)

    Discriminant: \((-4)^2 - 4(1)(4) = 0\). One real (repeated) root.

  • Example 2: \(2x^2 + x + 1 = 0\)

    Discriminant: \(1^2 - 4(2)(1) = -7 < 0\). Two complex roots.

Applications in Physics:

Quadratic equations arise in diverse physics contexts.

  • Example 1: Projectile Motion

    The height \(h\) of a projectile at time \(t\) can be modeled by \(h(t) = ut - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\) (for vertical motion), a quadratic equation in \(t\).

  • Example 2: Equilibrium Position

    Finding the equilibrium position of a spring-mass system involves solving a quadratic equation representing the net force.
