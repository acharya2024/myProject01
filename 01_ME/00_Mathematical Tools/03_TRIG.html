Chapter 3: Trigonometry and Angles

Understanding angles and their measurement is fundamental to trigonometry and its applications in physics. Angles quantify rotations or turns and are used extensively in analyzing circular motion, oscillations, and wave phenomena.

Degrees and Radians:

Two common units for measuring angles are degrees and radians. A full circle is divided into 360 degrees (\(360^\circ\)). Radians, on the other hand, define an angle based on the arc length it subtends on a circle.

One radian (rad) is the angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc whose length is equal to the radius of the circle.

Conversion between Degrees and Radians:

Since the circumference of a circle with radius \(r\) is \(2\pi r\), there are \(2\pi\) radians in a full circle. Therefore, we have the following conversion relationship:

\( \qquad 360^\circ = 2\pi \, \text{rad} \)

This relationship can be used to convert between degrees and radians. For instance, to convert an angle from degrees to radians, multiply by \(\frac{\pi}{180}\). To convert from radians to degrees, multiply by \(\frac{180}{\pi}\). Also \(1 \, \text{rad} = 57.3^{\circ} \).

  • Example 1: Degrees to Radians

    Convert \(45^\circ\) to radians:

    \(\qquad 45^\circ \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{4} \, \text{rad} \)

  • Example 2: Radians to Degrees

    Convert \(\frac{3\pi}{2} \, \text{rad}\) to degrees:

    \(\qquad \frac{3\pi}{2} \times \frac{180}{\pi} = 270^\circ \)

The core of trigonometry lies in the trigonometric functions, which relate angles to the ratios of sides in right-angled triangles. These functions are sine (sin), cosine (cos), tangent (tan), cosecant (cosec), secant (sec), and cotangent (cot).

Definitions in Right-Angled Triangles:

For a given angle θ in a right-angled triangle:

\( \sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} \)
\( \cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}} \)
\( \tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}} \)
\( \csc(\theta) = \frac{\text{Hypotenuse}}{\text{Opposite}} = \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)} \)
\( \sec(\theta) = \frac{\text{Hypotenuse}}{\text{Adjacent}} = \frac{1}{\cos(\theta)} \)
\( \cot(\theta) = \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Opposite}} = \frac{1}{\tan(\theta)} \)

The Unit Circle:

We extend the definitions of trigonometric functions to any angle, even those beyond \(90^\circ\), by using the unit circle, a circle with radius 1 centered at the origin of a coordinate plane.

  • Example 1: Trig Functions of 45°

    In a right triangle where \(\theta = 45^\circ\), if the opposite and adjacent sides are 1 and the hypotenuse is \(\sqrt{2}\), then

    \(\sin(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \), \(\cos(45^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \), and \(\tan(45^\circ) = 1\).
  • Example 2: Trig Functions of 30° and 60°

    In a \(30-60-90\) triangle, with sides \(1, \sqrt{3}\) and 2.

    \(\sin(30^\circ) = \cos(60^\circ) = \frac{1}{2} \), \(\cos(30^\circ) = \sin(60^\circ) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \), and \(\tan(30^\circ) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\) and \(\tan(60^\circ)=\sqrt{3}\).
