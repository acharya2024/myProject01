Rolling

Motion without slipping

Rolling is a common type of motion we observe daily, where an object moves forward while also rotating about an axis. We define rolling of a rigid body as the motion on a surface without any relative slipping between the body and the surface. Rolling as a Combination of Rotation and Translation

Rolling can be considered a combination of rotational and translational motion. The body rotates about its own axis while the axis moves along a path, which may or may not be a straight line. It is clearly visible in the animation above that center of the wheel is moving horizontally as well as there occurs rotation around the center.

When the rolling body is observed from a frame of reference moving along with its center, the motion of the body appears appears as pure rotation. Most of the times, this moving frame simplifies the analysis of the body's motion during rolling. For a circular rigid body rolling on a stationary surface, following relations between linear and angular variables can be established:

  • Relation Between Linear and Angular Velocity

    For a circular body (such as a disc, ring, or sphere) rolling on a fixed surface without slipping, the point of contact—the bottom-most point—remains stationary with respect to the surface to prevent slipping. If the center of the body moves with a linear velocity \( V_{\text{o}} \) and the body rotates with an angular velocity \( \omega \), the velocity at the point of contact due to rotation is \( -\omega R \) (negative because it opposes the direction of \( V_{\text{o}} \). To ensure no slipping occurs, the net velocity at the point of contact must be zero:

    \(\qquad V_{\text{o}} - \omega R = 0 \)

    \( \Rightarrow\quad V_{\text{o}} = \omega R \)

    where \( R \) is the radius of the rolling body.

  • Relation Between Linear and Angular Acceleration

    Consider a body rolling with a center velocity \( V_{\text{o}} \) and angular velocity \( \omega \) at a given instant. If the body continues to roll without slipping and the center's velocity increases with a linear acceleration \( a_{\text{o}} \), while the angular velocity increases with an angular acceleration \( \alpha \), the rolling condition after a small time \( dt \) is expressed as:

    \( \qquad V_{\text{o}} + a_{\text{o}} dt = (\omega + \alpha dt) R \)

    Subtracting \( \omega R \) from both sides:

    \( \qquad a_{\text{o}} dt = \alpha R dt \)

    \( \Rightarrow\quad a_{\text{o}} = \alpha R \)

    This equation shows that the linear acceleration of the center of mass is directly proportional to the angular acceleration, with the radius \( R \) serving as the constant of proportionality.

    If the surface on which the body is rolling is itself moving, similar relationships hold relative to the moving surface. The rolling condition (\( V_{\text{rel}} = \omega R \) and \( a_{\text{rel}} = \alpha R \)) applies to the velocities and accelerations relative to the surface, where \( V_{\text{rel}} \ \) and \( a_{\text{rel}} \) are the linear velocity and acceleration of the body's center of mass relative to the moving surface.

    By understanding these relationships, we can analyze the motion of rolling bodies more effectively, whether they are wheels, balls, or any other objects exhibiting rolling motion.

To analyze the kinematics of rolling objects, we can begin by understanding the motion relative to a translating frame in which the object appears to be rotating. Then, by vectorially adding the effects of translation, we can understand the motion relative to the ground.

Velocity of various points in rolling objects:

Consider a rigid body like a ring, disc, or sphere rolling on the floor with a constant velocity of its center as \(V_{\text{o}}\). Let's mark four points on the body: A and C at the top and bottom, and B and D at the sides.

Relative to ground the body appears to be rolling. If we consider a new frame \(F\) of reference moving with velocity \(V_{\text{o}}\) along with the center of object, then relative to this frame \(F\) the body appears to be in pure rotation. This is illustrated in following animation:

We derive the velocities of these points using the equation:

\(\qquad \vec{V} = \vec{V}_{\text{translation}} + \vec{V}_{\text{rotation}}\)

This is described in the following table:

Particle Velocity
\(\vec{V}_{\text{translation}}\) \(\vec{V}_{\text{rotation}}\) \(\vec{V}_{\text{resultant}}\)
A \(V_{\text{o}}\hat{i}\) \(\omega R\hat{i}\) \((V_{\text{o}} + \omega R)\hat{i}\)
B \(V_{\text{o}}\hat{i}\) \(-\omega R\hat{j}\) \(V_{\text{o}}\hat{i} - \omega R\hat{j}\)
C \(V_{\text{o}}\hat{i}\) \(-\omega R\hat{i}\) \((V_{\text{o}} - \omega R)\hat{i}\)
D \(V_{\text{o}}\hat{i}\) \(\omega R\hat{j}\) \(V_{\text{o}}\hat{i} + \omega R\hat{j}\)

By analyzing the velocities in both the translating and rotating frames and combining them vectorially, we can fully understand the motion of each point on the rolling object relative to the ground.

Determination of velocity using instantaneous axis of rotation:

Since rolling without slipping can be considered as pure rotation about the instantaneous axis of rotation (IAR) at the point of contact, the velocity of any point on the rolling body can be determined by:

\(\qquad \vec{V} = \omega \times \vec{r}_{\text{IAR}}\)

where \(\vec{r}_{\text{IAR}}\) is the position vector of the point from the IAR.

Determination of acceleration of various points with constant velocity:

When the rolling object moves with constant velocity (\(a_{\text{o}} = 0\), \(\alpha = 0\)), the accelerations of points are due to centripetal acceleration only.

Determination of acceleration of various points with constant acceleration:

The accelerations of the points can be analyzed by adding the translational and rotational accelerations:

\(\qquad \vec{a} = \vec{a}_{\text{translation}} + \vec{a}_{\text{rotation}}\)

This relationship is shown in the following table:

Particle Acceleration
\(\vec{a}_{\text{translation}}\) \(\vec{a}_{\text{rotation}}\)
A \(a_{\text{o}}\hat{i}\) \(-\omega^2 R \hat{j} + \alpha R \hat{i}\)
B \(a_{\text{o}}\hat{i}\) \(-\omega^2 R \hat{i} - \alpha R \hat{j}\)
C \(a_{\text{o}}\hat{i}\) \(\omega^2 R \hat{j} - \alpha R \hat{i}\)
D \(a_{\text{o}}\hat{i}\) \(\omega^2 R \hat{i} + \alpha R \hat{j}\)

Path traced by a circumference point of rolling object:

The path traced by a point on the circumference of a rolling object is called a cycloid. This path can be analyzed by considering the combination of rotational and translational motion.

Path traced by any general point of rolling object:

The path traced by any point on the rolling object can be determined by analyzing its motion relative to the center of mass and combining it with the translational motion of the center of mass.

Understanding requirement and direction of friction:

For a body to roll without slipping, friction is required between the surface and the rolling body. The frictional force provides the torque necessary for rotation. The direction of friction depends on whether the rolling body is accelerating or decelerating.

Development of Newton's law equation:

Applying Newton's second law to the center of mass:

\(\qquad \sum F = m a_{\text{CM}}\)

where \(\sum F\) is the net external force, \(m\) is the mass, and \(a_{\text{CM}}\) is the acceleration of the center of mass.

Development of torque equation relative to center of mass frame:

Applying rotational dynamics about the center of mass:

\(\qquad \sum \tau_{\text{CM}} = I_{\text{CM}} \alpha\)

where \(\sum \tau_{\text{CM}}\) is the net torque about the center of mass, \(I_{\text{CM}}\) is the moment of inertia about the center of mass, and \(\alpha\) is the angular acceleration.

Development of torque equation relative to instantaneous axis of rotation:

Alternatively, we can consider rotation about the instantaneous axis of rotation (IAR):

\(\qquad \sum \tau_{\text{IAR}} = I_{\text{IAR}} \alpha\)

Application of equations to find acceleration:

By combining the equations of motion and torque, we can solve for the acceleration \(a_{\text{o}}\) and angular acceleration \(\alpha\) of the rolling body.

Dynamics of rolling on inclined plane:

When a body rolls down an inclined plane, the component of gravitational force along the plane causes acceleration. Friction provides the torque for rotation.

Dynamics of rolling on movable plank:

In more complex scenarios, such as rolling on a movable plank, we need to consider the motion of both the rolling body and the plank, applying conservation of momentum and Newton's laws appropriately.

To understand the dynamics, we draw the free body diagram showing all the forces acting on the body, including friction.

We can then apply Newton's laws of motion and the equations of rotational dynamics to set up the following equations:

\(\qquad \sum F = m a_{\text{CM}}\)

\(\qquad \sum \tau = I \alpha\)

This helps in deriving the necessary equations to find various parameters like acceleration and angular acceleration, thus providing a detailed understanding of the dynamics of rolling.
