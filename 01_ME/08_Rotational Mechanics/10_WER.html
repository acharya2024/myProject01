Work and Energy in Rotation

To apply energy conservation

Rotational kinetic energy is the energy possessed by a rotating object due to its rotational motion. It is analogous to the kinetic energy of an object moving in a straight line, but for rotational motion. Just like the kinetic energy in linear motion depends on the mass and speed of an object, rotational kinetic energy depends on the moment of inertia and the angular velocity of the object.

For a rigid body of moment of inertia \(I\) rotating with an angular velocity \( \omega \) about an axis, the rotational kinetic energy is given by:

\(\qquad KE_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2 \)

This formula shows that rotational kinetic energy depends on both the distribution of mass (through the moment of inertia) and how fast the object is spinning (through the angular velocity). Just like mass in translational motion, moment of inertia plays a crucial role in determining how much energy is stored in the rotational motion.

  • Derivation: Kinetic energy of a rotating rigid body.

    Consider a rigid body consisting of multiple particles rotating about a fixed axis. Each particle has a mass \( m_i \) and is located at a distance \( r_i \) from the axis. The velocity of each particle due to the rotational motion is \( v_i = r_i \omega \), where \( \omega \) is the angular velocity of the entire rigid body.

    The kinetic energy of each particle is:

    \(\qquad KE_i = \frac{1}{2} m_i v_i^2 = \frac{1}{2} m_i (r_i \omega)^2 = \frac{1}{2} m_i r_i^2 \omega^2\)

    The total kinetic energy of the body is the sum of the kinetic energies of all its particles:

    \(\qquad KE_{\text{rot}} = \sum \frac{1}{2} m_i r_i^2 \omega^2\)

    Factoring out \( \omega^2 \), we get:

    \(\qquad KE_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} \omega^2 \sum m_i r_i^2\)

    The term \( \sum m_i r_i^2 \) is the moment of inertia \( I \) of the body about the axis of rotation. Therefore, the rotational kinetic energy simplifies to:

    \(\qquad KE_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2\)

Example: For a disc in rotation, the Rotational Kinetic Energy can be calculated as following:

Consider a uniform solid disc of mass \( M \) and radius \( R \), rotating about an axis through its center with an angular velocity \( \omega \). The moment of inertia of the disc about its center is given by:

\(\qquad I = \frac{1}{2} M R^2\)

The rotational kinetic energy is:

\(\qquad KE_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2} I \omega^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} M R^2 \times \omega^2 = \frac{1}{4} M R^2 \omega^2\)
