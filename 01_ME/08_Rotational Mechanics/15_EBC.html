Extended Body Collisions

Angular momentum conservation included

Rigid body collisions occur when solid bodies collide and exchange forces, causing changes in both their translational and rotational motion. These collisions can be analyzed using conservation laws depending on the type of collision and constraints of the system.

Conservation Principles in Rigid Body Collisions

During a rigid body collision, the following quantities may be conserved:

  • Linear Momentum: The total linear momentum is conserved if there are no external forces acting on the system.
  • Angular Momentum: The total angular momentum is conserved if no external torques are present.
  • Energy: Kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions.

In many cases, linear momentum may not be conserved due to external forces (such as friction or a fixed pivot). Angular momentum is often conserved because these forces typically do not generate torque about the point of collision.

  • Example 1: Collision of a Moving Ball with a Pivoted Rod

    Consider a ball of mass \( m \) moving horizontally with velocity \( v \) that strikes the end of a stationary rod of mass \( M \) and length \( L \), pivoted at one end. The ball sticks to the rod after the collision. Find the angular velocity \( \omega \) of the system immediately after the collision.

    Solution:

    We use conservation of angular momentum about the pivot point because there is no external torque about this point. Linear momentum is not conserved because the pivot exerts an external force, stopping the translational motion of the system.

    Initial angular momentum \( L_{\text{initial}} \) of the ball about the pivot:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{initial}} = m v L\)

    After the collision, the ball and rod rotate together. The total moment of inertia \( I_{\text{total}} \) about the pivot is:

    \(\qquad I_{\text{total}} = I_{\text{rod}} + I_{\text{ball}}\)

    Where:

    • \( I_{\text{rod}} = \dfrac{1}{3} M L^2 \) (moment of inertia of a rod about one end)
    • \( I_{\text{ball}} = m L^2 \) (moment of inertia of the ball at distance \( L \) from the pivot)

    Therefore:

    \(\qquad I_{\text{total}} = \left( \dfrac{1}{3} M + m \right) L^2\)

    Final angular momentum \( L_{\text{final}} \):

    \(\qquad L_{\text{final}} = I_{\text{total}} \omega\)

    Setting \( L_{\text{initial}} = L_{\text{final}} \) to conserve angular momentum:

    \(\qquad m v L = \left( \dfrac{1}{3} M + m \right) L^2 \omega\)

    Solving for \( \omega \):

    \(\qquad \omega = \dfrac{m v}{\left( \dfrac{1}{3} M + m \right) L}\)

  • Example 2: Collision of a Particle with a Freely Rotating Disc

    Consider a uniform disc of mass \( M \) and radius \( R \), which is free to rotate about an axis through its center and perpendicular to its plane. A small particle of mass \( m \), moving tangentially with velocity \( v \), collides with the edge of the disc and sticks to it. Find the angular velocity of system after the collision.

    Solution:

    Before the collision, the disc is stationary, and only the particle has linear momentum. After the collision, the particle sticks to the disc, causing the system (disc + particle) to rotate about the central axis of the disc.

    We apply the following conservation law:

    • Conservation of Angular Momentum: Since there is no external torque acting on the system, angular momentum is conserved about the center of the disc.

    Step 1: Initial Angular Momentum

    Before the collision, the particle has linear momentum \( mv \), which creates angular momentum about the center of the disc. The initial angular momentum of the system is:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{initial}} = m v R\)

    The disc is initially at rest, so its initial angular momentum is zero.

    Step 2: Final Angular Momentum

    After the collision, the particle sticks to the edge of the disc, and the whole system (disc + particle) rotates with angular velocity \( \omega \). The total angular momentum after the collision is:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{final}} = I_{\text{disc}} \omega + m R^2 \omega\)

    where \( I_{\text{disc}} = \frac{1}{2} M R^2 \) is the moment of inertia of the disc about its center, and \( m R^2 \) is the contribution to the moment of inertia from the particle at the edge of the disc.

    Step 3: Conservation of Angular Momentum

    Since angular momentum is conserved:

    \(\qquad m v R = \left( \frac{1}{2} M R^2 + m R^2 \right) \omega\)

    Simplifying for \( \omega \):

    \(\qquad \omega = \frac{m v}{\frac{1}{2} M R + m R}\)

    This example demonstrates how angular momentum conservation can be applied to a collision where a particle sticks to a rotating disc.

  • Example 3: Collision of a Translating Rod with a Fixed Obstacle

    A uniform rod of mass \( M = 2 \, \text{kg} \) and length \( L = 1 \, \text{m} \) is sliding on a frictionless horizontal table perpendicular to its length with a velocity \( v = 5 \, \text{m/s} \). The end of the rod suddenly hits a small fixed obstacle. Consider the collision to be perfectly inelastic causing the rod to start rotating about that end while the other end moves forward. Calculate:

    1. The angular velocity \( \omega \) of the rod immediately after the collision.
    2. The velocity \( v_{\text{cm}} \) of the center of mass immediately after the collision.

    Solution:

    When the rod's end collides with the fixed obstacle, an impulsive force acts at that point, causing the rod to rotate about the obstacle. Since the obstacle is fixed, external forces act, and linear momentum is not conserved. However, angular momentum about the point of collision is conserved because no external torque acts about this point during the collision.

    1. Conservation of Angular Momentum about the Point of Collision

    Before the collision, the rod is moving linearly with velocity \( v \). The initial angular momentum \( L_{\text{initial}} \) about the point of collision (one end of the rod) is:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{initial}} = M v \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right)\)

    After the collision, the rod rotates about the obstacle with angular velocity \( \omega \), and its center of mass moves forward with velocity \( v_{\text{cm}} \). The final angular momentum \( L_{\text{final}} \) is:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{final}} = I_{\text{end}} \omega\)

    Where \( I_{\text{end}} \) is the moment of inertia of the rod about one end:

    \(\qquad I_{\text{end}} = \dfrac{1}{3} M L^2\)

    Applying conservation of angular momentum:

    \(\qquad M v \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right) = \dfrac{1}{3} M L^2 \omega\)

    Solving for \( \omega \):

    \(\qquad \omega = \dfrac{3 v}{2 L}\)

    Substitute the given values:

    \(\qquad \omega = \dfrac{3 \times 5}{2 \times 1} = 7.5 \, \text{rad/s}\)

    2. Velocity of the Center of Mass After Collision

    The center of mass moves in a circular path about the point of collision with speed:

    \(\qquad v_{\text{cm}} = \omega \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right)\)

    Substitute the calculated \( \omega \):

    \(\qquad v_{\text{cm}} = 7.5 \times \dfrac{1}{2} = 3.75 \, \text{m/s}\)

    Answers:

    1. The angular velocity of the rod immediately after the collision is \( \omega = 7.5 \, \text{rad/s} \).
    2. The velocity of the center of mass immediately after the collision is \( v_{\text{cm}} = 3.75 \, \text{m/s} \).
  • Example 4: Collision of a Particle with a Free Rod on a Smooth Table

    A uniform rod of mass \( M = 3 \, \text{kg} \) and length \( L = 2 \, \text{m} \) is initially at rest on a smooth horizontal table. A particle of mass \( m = 1 \, \text{kg} \) moving horizontally with velocity \( v = 8 \, \text{m/s} \) collides elastically with the rod at a point \( \dfrac{L}{4} \) from one end. After the collision, the particle moves off in the opposite direction with velocity \( v' \). Calculate:

    1. The angular velocity \( \omega \) of the rod immediately after the collision.
    2. The velocity \( v_{\text{cm}} \) of the center of mass of the rod after the collision.
    3. The final velocity \( v' \) of the particle after the collision.

    Solution:

    Since the table is smooth, there are no external horizontal forces or torques acting on the system. Therefore, both linear momentum and angular momentum about the center of mass of the rod are conserved. Additionally, because the collision is elastic, kinetic energy is conserved.

    1. Conservation of Linear Momentum

    Total linear momentum before and after the collision:

    \(\qquad m v = m v' + M v_{\text{cm}}\)

    2. Conservation of Angular Momentum about the Rod's Center of Mass

    Distance from point of impact to the center of mass:

    \(\qquad r = \dfrac{L}{2} - \dfrac{L}{4} = \dfrac{L}{4}\)

    Angular momentum before collision:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{initial}} = m v \left( \dfrac{L}{4} \right)\)

    Angular momentum after collision:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{final}} = m v' \left( \dfrac{L}{4} \right) + I_{\text{cm}} \omega\)

    Moment of inertia of the rod about its center:

    \(\qquad I_{\text{cm}} = \dfrac{1}{12} M L^2\)

    Conservation of angular momentum:

    \(\qquad m v \left( \dfrac{L}{4} \right) = m v' \left( \dfrac{L}{4} \right) + \dfrac{1}{12} M L^2 \omega\)

    3. Conservation of Kinetic Energy

    \(\qquad \dfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = \dfrac{1}{2} m v'^2 + \dfrac{1}{2} M v_{\text{cm}}^2 + \dfrac{1}{2} I_{\text{cm}} \omega^2\)

    4. Solving the Equations

    From angular momentum conservation:

    \(\qquad m (v - v') \left( \dfrac{L}{4} \right) = \dfrac{1}{12} M L^2 \omega\)

    Simplify:

    \(\qquad \omega = \dfrac{3 m (v - v')}{M L}\)

    From linear momentum conservation:

    \(\qquad M v_{\text{cm}} = m (v - v')\)

    \(\qquad v_{\text{cm}} = \dfrac{m (v - v')}{M}\)

    Substitute \( v_{\text{cm}} \) and \( \omega \) into the kinetic energy equation and solve for \( v' \).

    After substituting values \( m = 1 \, \text{kg} \), \( M = 3 \, \text{kg} \), \( v = 8 \, \text{m/s} \), and \( L = 2 \, \text{m} \), we solve the equations and find:

    \(\qquad v' = -2.11 \, \text{m/s}\)

    \(\qquad v_{\text{cm}} = 3.37 \, \text{m/s}\)

    \(\qquad \omega = 5.05 \, \text{rad/s}\)

    Answers:

    1. The angular velocity of the rod after the collision is \( \omega \approx 5.05 \, \text{rad/s} \).
    2. The velocity of the rod's center of mass after the collision is \( v_{\text{cm}} \approx 3.37 \, \text{m/s} \).
    3. The final velocity of the particle after the collision is \( v' \approx -2.11 \, \text{m/s} \) (the particle reverses direction).
  • WRONG Example 5: Elastic Collision of a Translating Rod with a Fixed Obstacle

    A uniform rod of mass \( M = 2 \, \text{kg} \) and length \( L = 1 \, \text{m} \) is sliding on a frictionless horizontal table perpendicular to its length with a velocity \( v = 5 \, \text{m/s} \). The end of the rod collides elastically with a small fixed obstacle, causing the rod to rebound and rotate. Calculate:

    1. The angular velocity \( \omega \) of the rod immediately after the collision.
    2. The velocity \( v_{\text{cm}} \) of the center of mass immediately after the collision.

    Solution:

    In this elastic collision, mechanical energy and angular momentum about the point of collision are conserved. Linear momentum is not conserved due to the external force from the obstacle.

    1. Conservation of Angular Momentum about the Point of Collision

    Before the collision, the rod is moving linearly with velocity \( v \). The initial angular momentum \( L_{\text{initial}} \) about the point of collision (the end of the rod) is:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{initial}} = M v \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right)\)

    After the collision, the rod is rotating with angular velocity \( \omega \) and its center of mass moves backward with velocity \( v_{\text{cm}} \). The final angular momentum \( L_{\text{final}} \) is:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{final}} = I_{\text{end}} \omega - M v_{\text{cm}} \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right)\)

    The negative sign indicates that the center of mass is moving in the opposite direction after the collision.

    Moment of inertia of the rod about one end:

    \(\qquad I_{\text{end}} = \dfrac{1}{3} M L^2\)

    Applying conservation of angular momentum:

    \(\qquad M v \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right) = I_{\text{end}} \omega - M v_{\text{cm}} \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right)\)

    2. Conservation of Mechanical Energy

    Mechanical energy is conserved:

    \(\qquad \dfrac{1}{2} M v^2 = \dfrac{1}{2} I_{\text{end}} \omega^2 + \dfrac{1}{2} M v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    3. Solving the Equations

    We have two equations and two unknowns (\( \omega \) and \( v_{\text{cm}} \)). Solving them simultaneously gives:

    First, write the angular momentum equation:

    \(\qquad M v \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right) = \dfrac{1}{3} M L^2 \omega - M v_{\text{cm}} \left( \dfrac{L}{2} \right)\)

    Rewriting:

    \(\qquad \dfrac{M L}{2} (v + v_{\text{cm}}) = \dfrac{1}{3} M L^2 \omega\)

    Simplify mass \( M \) and length \( L \):

    \(\qquad \dfrac{v + v_{\text{cm}}}{2} = \dfrac{L}{3} \omega\)

    From energy conservation:

    \(\qquad \dfrac{1}{2} M v^2 = \dfrac{1}{2} \left( \dfrac{1}{3} M L^2 \right) \omega^2 + \dfrac{1}{2} M v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    Divide both sides by \( \dfrac{1}{2} M \):

    \(\qquad v^2 = \dfrac{1}{3} L^2 \omega^2 + v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    Now, express \( \omega \) in terms of \( v \) and \( v_{\text{cm}} \) from the first equation and substitute into the energy equation:

    \(\qquad \omega = \dfrac{3 (v + v_{\text{cm}})}{2 L}\)

    Substitute \( \omega \) into the energy equation:

    \(\qquad v^2 = \dfrac{1}{3} L^2 \left( \dfrac{3 (v + v_{\text{cm}})}{2 L} \right)^2 + v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    Simplify the equation and solve for \( v_{\text{cm}} \):

    \(\qquad v^2 = \dfrac{1}{3} \left( \dfrac{9 (v + v_{\text{cm}})^2}{4} \right) + v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    \(\qquad v^2 = \dfrac{3 (v + v_{\text{cm}})^2}{4} + v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    Multiply both sides by 4 to eliminate denominators:

    \(\qquad 4 v^2 = 3 (v + v_{\text{cm}})^2 + 4 v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    Expand and simplify:

    \(\qquad 4 v^2 = 3 (v^2 + 2 v v_{\text{cm}} + v_{\text{cm}}^2 ) + 4 v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    \(\qquad 4 v^2 = 3 v^2 + 6 v v_{\text{cm}} + 3 v_{\text{cm}}^2 + 4 v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    \(\qquad 4 v^2 - 3 v^2 = 6 v v_{\text{cm}} + 7 v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    \(\qquad v^2 = 6 v v_{\text{cm}} + 7 v_{\text{cm}}^2\)

    Bring all terms to one side:

    \(\qquad v^2 - 6 v v_{\text{cm}} - 7 v_{\text{cm}}^2 = 0\)

    This is a quadratic equation in \( v_{\text{cm}} \). Solving for \( v_{\text{cm}} \) gives \( v_{\text{cm}} = -v \).

    Therefore, \( v_{\text{cm}} = -5 \, \text{m/s} \) (the center of mass reverses direction).

    Now, find \( \omega \):

    \(\qquad \omega = \dfrac{3 (v + v_{\text{cm}})}{2 L} = \dfrac{3 (5 + (-5))}{2 \times 1} = 0\)

    But this suggests \( \omega = 0 \), which is inconsistent. The error arises because with \( v_{\text{cm}} = -v \), the rod doesn't rotate, which contradicts our initial assumption.

    Alternatively, solving the quadratic equation correctly:

    \(\qquad v^2 - 6 v v_{\text{cm}} - 7 v_{\text{cm}}^2 = 0\)

    Let’s use the quadratic formula. Let’s rearrange the equation:

    \(\qquad 7 v_{\text{cm}}^2 + 6 v v_{\text{cm}} - v^2 = 0\)

    Let’s denote \( x = v_{\text{cm}} \). Then the equation is:

    \(\qquad 7 x^2 + 6 v x - v^2 = 0\)

    Using the quadratic formula:

    \(\qquad x = \dfrac{ -6 v \pm \sqrt{ (6 v)^2 - 4 \times 7 \times (-v^2) } }{ 2 \times 7 }\)

    Simplify the discriminant:

    \(\qquad \Delta = 36 v^2 + 28 v^2 = 64 v^2\)

    Thus:

    \(\qquad x = \dfrac{ -6 v \pm 8 v }{14}\)

    First solution:

    \(\qquad x = \dfrac{ -6 v + 8 v }{14} = \dfrac{2 v}{14} = \dfrac{v}{7}\)

    Second solution:

    \(\qquad x = \dfrac{ -6 v - 8 v }{14} = \dfrac{ -14 v }{14} = -v\)

    So the two possible solutions for \( v_{\text{cm}} \) are \( v_{\text{cm}} = \dfrac{v}{7} \) and \( v_{\text{cm}} = -v \).

    The solution \( v_{\text{cm}} = -v \) corresponds to no rotation (\( \omega = 0 \)), which is not possible in this context since the rod must rotate after collision.

    Therefore, we take \( v_{\text{cm}} = \dfrac{v}{7} = \dfrac{5}{7} \, \text{m/s} \).

    Now, find \( \omega \):

    \(\qquad \omega = \dfrac{3 (v + v_{\text{cm}})}{2 L} = \dfrac{3 (5 + \dfrac{5}{7}) }{2 \times 1} = \dfrac{3 \times \dfrac{40}{7} }{2} = \dfrac{120}{14} = \dfrac{60}{7} \approx 8.57 \, \text{rad/s}\)

    Answers:

    1. The angular velocity of the rod immediately after the collision is \( \omega \approx 8.57 \, \text{rad/s} \).
    2. The velocity of the center of mass immediately after the collision is \( v_{\text{cm}} \approx 0.714 \, \text{m/s} \) in the same direction.
