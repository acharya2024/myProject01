Rotation with Translation

Both simultaneously

Translation and rotation are two fundamental types of motion. In translation, every part of an object moves along parallel paths, maintaining the same orientation. In rotation, all parts of the object move in circles around a common axis.

When an object experiences both translation and rotation simultaneously, we call this rotation with translation. The object moves forward while also spinning around an axis.

Examples include a spiraling stick thrown upward, which moves upward while rotating, or a football rolling across a field, moving forward while spinning. These are instances of rigid body motion involving both rotation and translation.

Understanding this combined motion is easier when viewed from a moving reference frame. Observing the object from a frame moving with it cancels out the translational motion, making the object appear to only rotate. For example, a cyclist perceives the bicycle wheels as only rotating because they move along with the bike.

Consider a rigid body undergoing both translation and rotation. Focus on a specific point \( P \) on the body, moving with velocity \( \vec{V}_P \) relative to the ground.

Since the body is rigid, all other points maintain constant distances from point \( P \), moving in circles around an axis passing through \( P \). This demonstrates pure rotational motion about point \( P \).

To analyze this motion, we introduce a reference frame \( F \) moving with velocity \( \vec{V}_P \), the same as point \( P \), but not rotating. In this frame, the body appears to only rotate about point \( P \). The velocity of any point \( Q \) relative to frame \( F \) is due solely to rotation:

\(\qquad \vec{V}_{\text{relative to }F} = \vec{V}_{\text{due to rotation}}\)

Here, \(\vec{V}_{\text{relative to }F}\) represents the velocity of point \( Q \) relative to frame \( F \).

The total velocity of point \( Q \) relative to the ground is the sum of the velocity of frame \( F \) and the velocity of \( Q \) relative to \( F \):

\(\qquad \vec{V}_{\text{relative to ground}} = \vec{V}_{\text{frame } F} + \vec{V}_{\text{relative to } F}\)

Substituting the earlier expression, we get:

\(\qquad \vec{V}_{\text{relative to ground}} = \vec{V}_{\text{due to translation}} + \vec{V}_{\text{due to rotation}}\)

The Instantaneous Axis of Rotation (IAR) is a fundamental concept in the kinematics of rigid body motion. At any given instant, the general plane motion of a rigid body—which includes both translational and rotational components—can be represented as a pure rotation about a unique axis known as the IAR.

This axis possesses several important properties:

  • Kinematic Equivalence to Pure Rotation:

    At any instant, the motion of a rigid body is equivalent to a pure rotation about the IAR with the instantaneous angular velocity \( \vec{\omega} \). The velocity \( \vec{v}_P \) of any point \( P \) on the body is given by:

    \(\qquad \vec{v}_P = \vec{\omega} \times \vec{r}_{P/I} \)

    where \( \vec{r}_{P/I} \) is the position vector of point \( P \) relative to the IAR.

  • Zero Velocity at the IAR:

    Points lying on the IAR have zero instantaneous velocity relative to the chosen inertial reference frame. Mathematically, if point \( I \) lies on the IAR, then:

    \(\qquad \vec{v}_I = \vec{0} \)

    This property is crucial for identifying the IAR by locating points of zero velocity.

  • Perpendicularity of Velocity Vectors:

    The velocity of any point \( P \) is perpendicular to the line joining \( P \) to the IAR. That is:

    \(\qquad \vec{v}_P \cdot \vec{r}_{P/I} = 0 \)

    This property aids in the geometric construction of the IAR using known velocity directions.

  • Instantaneous Nature:

    The IAR is unique at any given instant but may change position and orientation over time as the body's motion evolves. It captures the instantaneous rotational behavior of the body, even in complex motions involving both rotation and translation.

These properties lead to various methods for finding the IAR:

  • Method 1: Finding IAR Using Velocities of Two Points

    If the velocities of two distinct points \( A \) and \( B \) on the rigid body are known, the IAR can be found by constructing perpendiculars to the velocity vectors at these points. The intersection of these perpendiculars locates the IAR.

    Mathematically, since \( \vec{v}_A \cdot \vec{r}_{A/I} = 0 \) and \( \vec{v}_B \cdot \vec{r}_{B/I} = 0 \), the IAR lies at the intersection point where these conditions are satisfied.

  • Method 2: Finding IAR by Locating a Point at Rest

    If there is a point on the body that is instantaneously at rest, that point lies on the IAR. For example, in the case of a wheel rolling without slipping, the point of contact with the ground is momentarily at rest and thus lies on the IAR.

  • Method 3: Finding IAR Using Circular Paths

    If a point on the body is moving along a known circular path, the center of that circle is the IAR at that instant. The velocity of the point is tangential to the circle, and the line from the IAR to the point is the radius.

  • Method 4: Finding IAR with Parallel or Anti-Parallel Velocities

    When two points have velocities that are parallel or anti-parallel, the IAR can be found at infinity (indicating pure translation) or along the perpendicular bisector between the points, respectively.

Understanding and locating the Instantaneous Axis of Rotation simplifies the analysis of complex motions by reducing them to pure rotational motion about a single axis at each instant.

When a rigid body undergoes both translational and rotational motion, its total kinetic energy is the sum of its translational kinetic energy and rotational kinetic energy. Understanding how to calculate this combined kinetic energy is essential in analyzing the motion of objects experiencing complex movements.

The total kinetic energy \( KE_{\text{total}} \) of a rigid body undergoing both translation and rotation is given by:

\(\qquad KE_{\text{total}} = KE_{\text{translation}} + KE_{\text{rotation}} = \frac{1}{2} M V_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2} I_{\text{cm}} \omega^2\)

Where:

  • \( M \): Mass of the rigid body
  • \( V_{\text{cm}} \): Velocity of the center of mass
  • \( I_{\text{cm}} \): Moment of inertia about the center of mass
  • \( \omega \): Angular velocity about the center of mass

This formula shows that the total kinetic energy is simply the sum of the kinetic energies due to translation of center of mass and rotation of the body around the center of mass. The derivation of this result is provided below:

  • Derivation of the Total Kinetic Energy Formula

    To determine the total kinetic energy of a rigid body undergoing both translational and rotational motion, we consider the contributions from each infinitesimal mass element \( dm \) that makes up the body.

    Each mass element has a velocity \( \vec{v} \) that can be decomposed into two components:

    \(\qquad \vec{v} = \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{v}'\)

    where:

    • \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \) is the velocity of the center of mass.
    • \( \vec{v}' \) is the velocity of the mass element relative to the center of mass.

    Since the rigid body undergoes pure rotation about its center of mass, the relative velocity \( \vec{v}' \) is given by:

    \(\qquad \vec{v}' = \vec{\omega} \times \vec{r}'\)

    where:

    • \( \vec{\omega} \) is the angular velocity vector.
    • \( \vec{r}' \) is the position vector of the mass element relative to the center of mass.

    The kinetic energy \( dKE \) of each mass element \( dm \) is:

    \(\qquad dKE = \frac{1}{2} dm \, v^2\)

    Substituting the expression for \( \vec{v} \):

    \(\qquad dKE = \frac{1}{2} dm \left( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{v}' \right) \cdot \left( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{v}' \right)\)

    Expanding the dot product, we get:

    \(\qquad dKE = \frac{1}{2} dm \left( V_{\text{cm}}^2 + 2 \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \cdot \vec{v}' + v'^2 \right)\)

    Integrating over the entire mass of the rigid body, the total kinetic energy is:

    \(\qquad KE_{\text{total}} = \int dKE = \frac{1}{2} M V_{\text{cm}}^2 + \int dm \, \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \cdot \vec{v}' + \frac{1}{2} \int dm \, v'^2\)

    Let's analyze each term individually:

    First Term: Translational Kinetic Energy

    The first term, \( \frac{1}{2} M V_{\text{cm}}^2 \), represents the kinetic energy of the entire rigid body as if all its mass were concentrated at the center of mass moving with velocity \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \). This is known as the translational kinetic energy of the body.

    Second Term: Dot product term

    The second term involves the integral \( \int dm \, \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \cdot \vec{v}' \). Since \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \) is constant with respect to the mass elements, we can take it outside the integral:

    \(\qquad \int dm \, \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \cdot \vec{v}' = \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \cdot \left( \int dm \, \vec{v}' \right)\)

    The integral \( \int dm \, \vec{v}' \) represents the total momentum of the system relative to the center of mass frame. In the center of mass frame, total momentum of any system of particles remains zero. Therefore,:

    \(\qquad \int dm \, \vec{v}' = \vec{0}\)

    Therefore, the second term vanishes:

    \(\qquad \int dm \, \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \cdot \vec{v}' = \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \cdot \vec{0} = 0\)

    Third Term: Rotational Kinetic Energy

    The third term involves the integral \( \frac{1}{2} \int dm \, v'^2 \), which represents the sum of the kinetic energies of all mass elements due to their motion relative to the center of mass. Since the only possible motion of the rigid body relative to the center of mass is pure rotation, this term corresponds to the rotational kinetic energy about the center of mass.

    We can express this term as:

    \(\qquad \frac{1}{2} \int dm \, v'^2 =\frac{1}{2} I_{\text{cm}} \omega^2\)

    where \( I_{\text{cm}} \) is the moment of inertia of the body about the center of mass.

    Final Expression for Total Kinetic Energy

    Combining all the terms, the total kinetic energy of the rigid body is:

    \(\qquad KE_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2} M V_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2} I_{\text{cm}} \omega^2\)

    This expression shows that the total kinetic energy is the sum of:

    • Translational Kinetic Energy: \( \frac{1}{2} M V_{\text{cm}}^2 \), due to the motion of the center of mass. In other words, the kinetic energy of body as if the body is concentrated at its center of mass
    • Rotational Kinetic Energy: \( \frac{1}{2} I_{\text{cm}} \omega^2 \), due to rotation about the center of mass. In other words, the kinetic energy of the body relative to its center of mass frame.

    Therefore, when analyzing the kinetic energy of a rigid body in combined translational and rotational motion, we can treat the translational and rotational components separately and sum their energies to find the total kinetic energy.

Let's explore this concept with an example:

    • Example 1: Kinetic Energy of a Frisbee Thrown with Spin

      A frisbee of mass 0.2 kg and radius 0.15 m is thrown with a forward velocity of 5 m/s while spinning with an angular velocity of 20 rad/s. Calculate its total kinetic energy after it is thrown.

      Solution:

      The frisbee has both translational and rotational motion, so its total kinetic energy is the sum of translational and rotational kinetic energies.

      Translational Kinetic Energy:

      \(\qquad KE_{\text{translation}} = \frac{1}{2} M V_{\text{cm}}^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.2 \times (5)^2 = 2.5 \, \text{J}\)

      Rotational Kinetic Energy:

      The frisbee spins about its center, so the rotational kinetic energy is:

      \(\qquad KE_{\text{rotation}} = \frac{1}{2} I_{\text{cm}} \omega^2\)

      For a flat disc (the shape of a frisbee), the moment of inertia is:

      \(\qquad I_{\text{cm}} = \frac{1}{2} M R^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.2 \times (0.15)^2 = 0.00225 \, \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\)

      Therefore:

      \(\qquad KE_{\text{rotation}} = \frac{1}{2} \times 0.00225 \times (20)^2 = 0.45 \, \text{J}\)

      Total Kinetic Energy:

      \(\qquad KE_{\text{total}} = KE_{\text{translation}} + KE_{\text{rotation}} = 2.5 + 0.45 = 2.95 \, \text{J}\)

      Therefore, the total kinetic energy of the frisbee after it is thrown is \( 2.95 \, \text{J} \).

This approach can be applied to any rigid body undergoing both translation and rotation, allowing for comprehensive analysis of its kinetic energy.

When a rigid body undergoes both translational and rotational motion, its total angular momentum is the sum of its angular momentum due to translation and rotation. Understanding how to calculate this combined angular momentum is essential in analyzing the motion of objects experiencing complex movements.

The total angular momentum \( \vec{L}_{\text{total}} \) of a rigid body in rotation with translation about a fixed point \( O \) is given by:

\(\qquad \vec{L}_{\text{total}} = \vec{L}_{\text{translation}} + \vec{L}_{\text{rotation}} = \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times (M \vec{V}_{\text{cm}}) + I_{\text{cm}} \vec{\omega}\)

Where:

  • \( M \): Mass of the rigid body
  • \( \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \): Position vector of the center of mass relative to point \( O \)
  • \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \): Velocity of the center of mass
  • \( I_{\text{cm}} \): Moment of inertia about the center of mass
  • \( \vec{\omega} \): Angular velocity around the axis through the center of mass

This formula shows that the total angular momentum is the sum of the angular momentum due to the motion of the center of mass and the angular momentum due to rotation about the center of mass. The derivation of this result is provided below:

  • Derivation of the Total Angular Momentum Formula

    The total angular momentum \( \vec{L}_{\text{total}} \) is obtained by calculating the angular momentum of each mass element \( dm \) in the body. The position vector of each mass element relative to a fixed point \( O \) is given by:

    \(\qquad \vec{r} = \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{r}'\)

    where \( \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \) is the position of the center of mass relative to point \( O \), and \( \vec{r}' \) is the position of the mass element relative to the center of mass.

    The velocity of each mass element can similarly be expressed as:

    \(\qquad \vec{v} = \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{v}'\)

    where \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \) is the velocity of the center of mass, and \( \vec{v}' \) is the velocity of the mass element relative to the center of mass. For a rigid body undergoing pure rotation about its center of mass, \( \vec{v}' \) is related to the angular velocity \( \vec{\omega} \) by:

    \(\qquad \vec{v}' = \vec{\omega} \times \vec{r}'\)

    The total angular momentum of the rigid body is given by:

    \(\qquad \vec{L}_{\text{total}} = \int \vec{r} \times \vec{v} \, dm = \int (\vec{r}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{r}') \times (\vec{V}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{v}') \, dm\)

    Expanding the cross product:

    \(\qquad \vec{L}_{\text{total}} = \int \left[ \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times \vec{v}' + \vec{r}' \times \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} + \vec{r}' \times \vec{v}' \right] \, dm\)

    Now, let's simplify each term individually:

    • Term 1: \( \int \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \, dm \)

      • Since \( \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \) and \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \) are constants with respect to \( dm \), this term simplifies to:

      \(\qquad \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \int dm = \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times (M \vec{V}_{\text{cm}})\)

      This represents the angular momentum due to the translation of the center of mass. In other words, it represents the angular momentum of the system considering entire system to be concentrated at its center of mass.

    • Term 2: \( \int \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times \vec{v}' \, dm \)

      • Since \( \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \) is constant, we can take it outside the integral:

      \(\qquad \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times \int \vec{v}' \, dm\)

      The integral \( \int \vec{v}' \, dm \) represents the total momentum of the system relative to the center of mass, which is zero. Thus, this term is:

      \(\qquad \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times \vec{0} = \vec{0}\)

      This term vanishes because the total momentum in the center of mass frame is zero.

    • Term 3: \( \int \vec{r}' \times \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \, dm \)

      • Since \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \) is constant, this term becomes:

      \(\qquad \left( \int \vec{r}' \, dm \right) \times \vec{V}_{\text{cm}}\)

      This also becomes zero because the integral \( \int \vec{r}' \, dm \) represents the sum of the position vectors \( \vec{r}' \) of all mass elements relative to the center of mass. This effectively measures the location of the center of mass from the center of mass itself, which is zero by definition. Therefore, this term vanishes:

      \(\qquad \int \vec{r}' \times \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \, dm = \vec{0} \)

    • Term 4: \( \int \vec{r}' \times \vec{v}' \, dm \)

      • This term represents the angular momentum of the rigid body relative to the center of mass. Since a rigid body can only perform pure rotation if observed relative to center of mass, the angular momentum relative to the center of mass is given by:

      \(\qquad \vec{L}'_{\text{rotation}} = I_{\text{cm}} \vec{\omega}\)

      where \( I_{\text{cm}} \) is the moment of inertia about the center of mass.

      Thus, this term simplifies to:

      \(\qquad \int \vec{r}' \times \vec{v}' \, dm = I_{\text{cm}} \vec{\omega}\)

    Now, combining all the terms, we have:

    \(\qquad \vec{L}_{\text{total}} = \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times (M \vec{V}_{\text{cm}}) + I_{\text{cm}} \vec{\omega} \)

    The first term, \( \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times (M \vec{V}_{\text{cm}}) \), represents the angular momentum due to the translational motion of the center of mass, where \( M \) is the total mass of the rigid body, \( \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \) is the position of the center of mass relative to point \( O \), and \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \) is the velocity of the center of mass.

    The second term, \( I_{\text{cm}} \vec{\omega} \), represents the angular momentum due to the rotation of the rigid body about its center of mass, where \( I_{\text{cm}} \) is the moment of inertia of the body about its center of mass, and \( \vec{\omega} \) is the angular velocity of the body about its center of mass.

    This result for angular momentum is analogous to the formula for kinetic energy in rotation with translation. In the case of kinetic energy, the total energy is a scalar sum of the translational kinetic energy \( \frac{1}{2} M V_{\text{cm}}^2 \) and the rotational kinetic energy \( \frac{1}{2} I_{\text{cm}} \omega^2 \). However, for angular momentum, it is a vector sum of the angular momentum due to translation \( \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times (M \vec{V}_{\text{cm}}) \) and the angular momentum due to rotation \( I_{\text{cm}} \vec{\omega} \). This vector nature of angular momentum means one must be careful when applying this formula, as the directions of the angular momentum components matter, unlike the scalar quantities in kinetic energy, which only require magnitudes.

Let's explore this concept with an example:

  • Example 1: Angular Momentum of a Disc Translating and Rotating

    A solid disc of mass 10 kg and radius 0.5 m is moving on a horizontal surface with a linear velocity of 4 m/s while also rotating with an angular velocity \(8\text{rad/s}\). Calculate its total angular momentum about a fixed point \( O \) on the surface directly below the center of the disc at a given instant.

    Solution:

    The total angular momentum about point \( O \) is the sum of the angular momentum due to translation and the angular momentum due to rotation about the center of mass.

    Angular Momentum Due to Translation:

    \(\qquad \vec{L}_{\text{translation}} = M (\vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \times \vec{V}_{\text{cm}}) \)

    At the given instant, \( \vec{r}_{\text{cm}} \) is upward with a magnitude of 0.5 m, and \( \vec{V}_{\text{cm}} \) is horizontal with a magnitude of 4 m/s. Therefore, the magnitude of \( \vec{L}_{\text{translation}} \) is:

    \(\qquad L_{\text{translation}} = M R V_{\text{cm}} = 10 \times 0.5 \times 4 = 20 \, \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s} \)

    Direction: Into the plane (using the right-hand rule).

    Angular Momentum Due to Rotation:

    Moment of inertia of a solid disc about its center of mass is:

    \(\qquad I_{\text{cm}} = \frac{1}{2} M R^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 10 \times (0.5)^2 = 1.25 \, \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2 \)

    Therefore, the angular momentum due to rotation is:

    \(\qquad \vec{L}_{\text{rotation}} = I_{\text{cm}} \vec{\omega} = 1.25 \times \frac{4}{0.5} = 10 \, \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s} \)

    Direction: Into the plane (same as \( \vec{L}_{\text{translation}} \)).

    Total Angular Momentum:

    \(\qquad \vec{L}_{\text{total}} = \vec{L}_{\text{translation}} + \vec{L}_{\text{rotation}} = 20 + 10 = 30 \, \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s} \)

    Therefore, the total angular momentum of the disc about point \( O \) is \( 30 \, \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2/\text{s} \), directed into the plane.

This approach can be applied to any rigid body undergoing both translation and rotation, allowing for comprehensive analysis of its angular momentum in various mechanical systems.
