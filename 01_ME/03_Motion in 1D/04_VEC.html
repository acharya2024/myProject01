Vectors

Mathematical Toolkit for Physics

In physics, we often deal with quantities that have both magnitude and direction. These quantities are called vectors. Understanding vectors is crucial for describing motion, forces, and many other physical phenomena.

Vectors vs. Scalars:

  • Vectors: Quantities with both magnitude and direction. Examples include:
    • Displacement: A change in position (e.g., 5 meters east).
    • Velocity: The rate of change of position (e.g., 10 m/s north).
    • Force: A push or pull (e.g., 20 Newtons downward).
  • Scalars: Quantities with only magnitude. Examples include:
    • Distance: The total length traveled (e.g., 5 meters).
    • Speed: The rate of travel (e.g., 10 m/s).
    • Mass: The amount of matter in an object (e.g., 2 kg).

Geometric Representation:

We can represent a vector graphically as an arrow. The length of the arrow represents the magnitude of the vector, and the direction the arrow points represents the direction of the vector.

[Diagram_Here: A labeled arrow representing a vector, showing magnitude and direction.]

Notation:

There are several ways to represent vectors in writing:

  • Boldface: Using boldface letters (e.g., \(\vec{A}\)).
  • Arrow over letter: Drawing an arrow above the letter (e.g., \(\overrightarrow{A}\)).

Unit Vectors

A unit vector is a vector with a magnitude of exactly 1. Unit vectors are used to specify a direction. We will use the "hat" notation, such as \(\hat{A}\) to indicate a unit vector.

[Diagram_Here: A unit vector with the formula \(\hat{A}\) = \(\dfrac{A}{|A|}\) ]

We define \( \hat{i} \) to be a unit vector pointing in the positive x direction, \( \hat{j} \) to be a unit vector pointing in the positive y direction, and \( \hat{k} \) to be a unit vector pointing in the positive z direction.

[Diagram_Here: i,j and k unit vector pointing to their axes. ]

These unit vectors are crucial for expressing vectors in component form, which we'll discuss later.

A. Equality of Vectors

Two vectors, \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\), are equal if and only if they have the same magnitude and the same direction.

[Diagram_Here: Two vectors, A and B, of equal length and pointing in the same direction.]

B. Vector Addition

Vector addition combines two or more vectors into a single resultant vector.

  • Geometric Methods:
    • Triangle Law: Place the tail of vector \(\vec{B}\) at the head of vector \(\vec{A}\). The resultant vector R is the vector from the tail of \(\vec{A}\) to the head of \(\vec{B}\).

      • [Diagram_Here: Triangle law of vector addition with vectors A, B, and R labeled.]

    • Parallelogram Law: Place the tails of vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) at the same point. Complete the parallelogram formed by \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\). The resultant vector R is the diagonal of the parallelogram starting from the tails of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\).

      • [Diagram_Here: Parallelogram law of vector addition with vectors A, B, and R labeled.]

    • Polygon Law: For adding multiple vectors, place the vectors head-to-tail. The resultant vector is from the tail of the first vector to the head of the last vector.

      • [Diagram_Here: Polygon law of vector addition with multiple vectors and the resultant vector labeled.]

  • Component Method:

    We will discuss how to add vectors using components in a later section, after we've covered vector decomposition.

Resolving a vector into its components is a fundamental technique that simplifies many vector calculations. It involves breaking down a vector into two or more vectors that are perpendicular to each other (usually along the x and y axes).

Finding Components Given Magnitude and Direction:

If we know the magnitude (A) of a vector \(\vec{A}\) and the angle (θ) it makes with the positive x-axis, we can find its x and y components using the following equations:

\( \qquad A_x = A \cos \theta \)

\( \qquad A_y = A \sin \theta \)

[Diagram_Here: A vector A, its x-component Ax, its y component Ay, and the angle θ with respect to x-axis. ]

Finding Magnitude and Direction Given Components:

Conversely, if we know the x and y components of a vector, we can find its magnitude and direction using the following equations:

\( \qquad A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2} \)

\( \qquad \theta = \arctan\left(\dfrac{A_y}{A_x}\right) \)

Important Note: The arctangent function (tan-1) only gives angles in the range of -90° to +90°. You need to consider the signs of \(A_x\) and \(A_y\) to determine the correct quadrant for the angle \(\theta\).

Decomposition of vectors into their x, y and z-components:

In three dimensions, a vector A can be expressed as:

\( \qquad \hat{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j} + A_z \hat{k} \)

where \(A_x\), \( A_y \) and \( A_z \) are the x-, y-, and z-components of vector A, respectively, and \( \hat{i} \), \( \hat{j} \), and \( \hat{k} \) are the unit vectors along the x-, y-, and z-axes, respectively.

[Diagram_Here: Decomposition of vectors in 3d world with all components labelled]

This decomposition allows us to analyze and manipulate vectors in three dimensions more easily.

A. Scalar Product (Dot Product)

The scalar product (or dot product) of two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is a scalar quantity defined as:

\( \qquad \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A B \cos \theta \)

where \(A\) and \(B\) are the magnitudes of the vectors, and \(\theta\) is the angle between them.

Geometric Interpretation:

The dot product can be interpreted as the product of the magnitude of one vector and the projection of the other vector onto it.

[Diagram_Here: Geometric interpretation of dot product as projection]

Component Form:

In terms of components, the dot product is given by:

\( \qquad \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y + A_z B_z \)

Properties:

  • Commutative: \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A}\)
  • Distributive: \(\vec{A} \cdot (\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} + \vec{A} \cdot \vec{C}\)

Applications:

  • Finding the angle between two vectors: \(\qquad \cos \theta = \dfrac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}}{A B}\)
  • Calculating work done by a force: \(W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}\)
Illustrative Examples: Dot Product
  • Example 1: Find the dot product of \(\vec{A} = 2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j}\) and \(\vec{B} = -\hat{i} + 2\hat{j}\)

    \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = (2)(-1) + (3)(2) = -2 + 6 = 4\)

  • Example 2: Find the angle between vectors \(\vec{A} = \hat{i} + \hat{j}\) and \(\vec{B} = \hat{i} - \hat{j}\)

    \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = (1)(1) + (1)(-1) = 0\). Since the dot product is 0, the vectors are orthogonal (perpendicular), so \(\theta = 90^\circ\).

  • Example 3: Calculate the work done by a force \(\vec{F} = 5\hat{i}\) N acting on an object that moves a displacement \(\vec{d} = 2\hat{i} + \hat{j}\) m

    \(W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d} = (5)(2) + (0)(1) = 10\) J

B. Vector Product (Cross Product)

The vector product (or cross product) of two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is a vector quantity whose magnitude is defined as:

\( \qquad |\vec{A} \times \vec{B}| = A B \sin \theta \)

where \(A\) and \(B\) are the magnitudes of the vectors, and \(\theta\) is the angle between them.

Direction:

The direction of \(\vec{A} \times \vec{B}\) is perpendicular to both \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\), and is determined by the right-hand rule. If you point the fingers of your right hand in the direction of \(\vec{A}\) and curl them towards \(\vec{B}\), your thumb will point in the direction of \(\vec{A} \times \vec{B}\).

[Diagram_Here: Right-hand rule diagram for cross product]

Component Form (in 3D):

If \(\vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} + A_y \hat{j} + A_z \hat{k}\) and \(\vec{B} = B_x \hat{i} + B_y \hat{j} + B_z \hat{k}\), then the cross product is given by the determinant:

\( \qquad \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ A_x & A_y & A_z \\ B_x & B_y & B_z \end{vmatrix} \)

Expanding the determinant:

\( \qquad \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = (A_y B_z - A_z B_y) \hat{i} - (A_x B_z - A_z B_x) \hat{j} + (A_x B_y - A_y B_x) \hat{k} \)

Properties:

  • Anti-commutative: \(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = -\vec{B} \times \vec{A}\)
  • Distributive: \(\vec{A} \times (\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = \vec{A} \times \vec{B} + \vec{A} \times \vec{C}\)

Applications:

  • Calculating torque: \(\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}\)
  • Calculating angular momentum: \(\vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p}\)
  • Finding the area of a parallelogram formed by two vectors: \(Area = |\vec{A} \times \vec{B}|\)
Illustrative Examples: Dot Product
  • Example 1: Find the cross product of \(\vec{A} = 2\hat{i} + 3\hat{j}\) and \(\vec{B} = -\hat{i} + 2\hat{j}\)

    \( \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 2 & 3 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = (0-0)\hat{i} + (0-0)\hat{j} + (4+3)\hat{k} = 7\hat{k}\)

  • Example 2: Find the Torque when force of \(\vec{F} = \hat{i} + \hat{j}\) is applied on position vector \(\vec{r} = \hat{i} - \hat{j}\)

    \( \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = (0-0)\hat{i} + (0-0)\hat{j} + (-1-1)\hat{k} = -2\hat{k}\)

  • Example 3: Find the area of a parallelogram created through vectors \(\vec{A} = \hat{i} + \hat{j}\) and \(\vec{B} = \hat{i} - \hat{j}\)

    Since the magnitude of the cross product gives area then = 2
