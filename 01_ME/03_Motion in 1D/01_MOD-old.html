Motion on Straight Line

Describing Linear Motion

Distance is a scalar quantity that measures the total length of the path traveled by an object. It depends on the actual path taken and does not consider direction. The measurement of distance requires a reference starting point and is associated with a specific time interval.

[Diagram: A curved path illustrating the total distance covered]

Average speed is the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken. It provides a single value that represents the overall rate of motion during the journey, irrespective of the path's shape or direction.

\( \text{Average Speed} = \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}} \)

For example, if a car travels 100 km in the first 2 hours and 150 km in the next 3 hours, the average speed during the journey is:

\(\qquad \text{Average Speed} = \frac{100 \, \text{km} + 150 \, \text{km}}{2 \, \text{h} + 3 \, \text{h}} \)

\(\Rightarrow\quad \text{Average Speed} = \frac{250 \, \text{km}}{5 \, \text{h}} = 50 \, \text{km/h} \)

This section focuses on solving problems related to average speed when the speed of a particle varies during its motion. Various cases are analyzed to illustrate the concept in different scenarios.

  • Case 1: Total distance and total time given

    If the total distance \(d\) and total time \(t\) are given, the average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{d}{t}\)

    Explanation: Average speed is the simplest measure of how fast something travels over a journey. It depends only on the total distance covered and the total time taken, regardless of variations in speed during the journey.

    Example: A car travels 100 km in 2 hours. The average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{100 \, \text{km}}{2 \, \text{h}} = 50 \, \text{km/h}\)

  • Case 2: Equal time intervals

    If a particle travels for equal time intervals \(t_1 = t_2 = \frac{t}{2}\) with speeds \(v_1\) and \(v_2\), the average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{v_1 t_1 + v_2 t_2}{t_1 + t_2} = \frac{v_1 + v_2}{2}\)

    Explanation: Since the time intervals are equal, the average speed is simply the arithmetic mean of the two speeds. This is because the time spent at each speed is identical.

    Example: A cyclist rides at 20 km/h for the first half hour and 40 km/h for the next half hour. The average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{20 + 40}{2} = 30 \, \text{km/h}\)

  • Case 3: Unequal time intervals

    If time intervals are unequal, \(t_1\) and \(t_2\), with speeds \(v_1\) and \(v_2\), the average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{v_1 t_1 + v_2 t_2}{t_1 + t_2}\)

    Explanation: In this case, the average speed takes into account the weighted contribution of each speed based on the time spent at that speed.

    Example: A car travels at 60 km/h for 2 hours and then at 80 km/h for 1 hour. The average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{60 \cdot 2 + 80 \cdot 1}{2 + 1} = \frac{120 + 80}{3} = 66.67 \, \text{km/h}\)

  • Case 4: Equal distances

    If distances are equal, \(d_1 = d_2 = \frac{d}{2}\), with speeds \(v_1\) and \(v_2\), the average speed becomes:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{d}{\frac{d/2}{v_1} + \frac{d/2}{v_2}} = \frac{2v_1 v_2}{v_1 + v_2}\)

    Explanation: When distances are equal, the time taken for each segment is inversely proportional to the speed. The average speed is the harmonic mean of the two speeds.

    Example: A person walks 1 km at 5 km/h and runs 1 km at 10 km/h. The average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{2 \cdot 5 \cdot 10}{5 + 10} = \frac{100}{15} = 6.67 \, \text{km/h}\)

  • Case 5: Unequal distances

    For unequal distances \(d_1\) and \(d_2\), covered with speeds \(v_1\) and \(v_2\), the average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{d_1 + d_2}{\frac{d_1}{v_1} + \frac{d_2}{v_2}}\)

    Explanation: Here, the average speed accounts for both the varying distances and the time taken for each segment.

    Example: A car travels 60 km at 80 km/h and 40 km at 40 km/h. The average speed is:

    \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{60 + 40}{\frac{60}{80} + \frac{40}{40}} = \frac{100}{0.75 + 1} = 57.14 \, \text{km/h}\)

Consider a particle moving according to the equation \(x = t^2\), where \(x\) represents the distance traveled, and \(t\) is the time.

Average speed from \(t=0\) to \(t=4s\)

\(\qquad x(0) = 0, \, x(4) = 16\)

\(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{x(4) - x(0)}{4 - 0} = \frac{16 - 0}{4 - 0} = 4 \, \text{units/s}\)

Average speed from \(t=2s\) to \(t=5s\)

\(\qquad x(2) = 4, \, x(5) = 25\)

\(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{x(5) - x(2)}{5 - 2} = \frac{25 - 4}{5 - 2} = 7 \, \text{units/s}\)

Speed at \(t=2s\)

To determine the speed at exactly \(t=2s\), calculate the average speeds over progressively smaller time intervals around \(t=2s\).

From \(t=2.0s\) to \(t=2.1s\): \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{x(2.1) - x(2.0)}{2.1 - 2.0} = \frac{2.1^2 - 2.0^2}{2.1 - 2.0} = 4.1 \, \text{units/s}\)

From \(t=2.00s\) to \(t=2.01s\): \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{x(2.01) - x(2.0)}{2.01 - 2.0} = \frac{2.01^2 - 2.0^2}{2.01 - 2.0} = 4.01 \, \text{units/s}\)

From \(t=2s\) to \(t=(2+h)s\): \(\qquad v_{avg} = \frac{(2+h)^2 - 2^2}{(2+h) - 2} = \frac{4h + h^2}{h} = 4 + h \, \text{units/s}\)

As \(h\) tends to zero, \(v_{avg}\) approaches \(4 \, \text{units/s}\). This limiting value is the instantaneous speed at \(t=2s\).

Instantaneous speed is the speed of an object at a particular instant of time. It's the limit of the average speed as the time interval approaches zero.

\( v_{ins} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt} \)

Here, \(\frac{dx}{dt}\) represents the derivative of \(x\) with respect to \(t\). It denotes the rate of change of position \(x\) with respect to time \(t\) at a given instant. Don't worry; it's simpler than it sounds!

Illustrative Examples
  • Example 1: \(x = 5t^3 + 2t + 7\)

    \(v_{ins} = \frac{dx}{dt} = 15t^2 + 2\)

  • Example 2: \(x = 2\sin(t) + 3\cos(2t)\)

    \( v_{ins} = \frac{dx}{dt} = 2\cos(t) - 6\sin(2t) \)

  • Example 3: \(x = 4e^{3t} + \ln(5t)\)

    \( v_{ins} = \frac{dx}{dt} = 12e^{3t} + \frac{1}{t}\)

  • Example 4: \(x = \frac{2}{t^3}\)

    \( v_{ins} = \frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{-6}{t^4} \)

  • Example 5: \( x= 5 \)

    \(v_{ins} = \frac{dx}{dt} = 0\) [As distance is constant, there is no motion.]

Displacement

Displacement is a vector quantity that represents the change in position of an object. It's the shortest distance between the initial and final positions, along with the direction.

[Diagram: Showing displacement vector between two points]

Example A person walks 10 m east and then walks 3 m in west direction. Total distance covered = \((10+3)=13m\) and displacement = \((+10 - 3)=+7m\). Here \(+ve \) represents direction along positive \(x\) axis.

Average Velocity

Average velocity is defined as the displacement divided by the total time taken.

\( \text{Average velocity}=\frac{ \text{Displacement}}{ \text{Total Time}} \)
