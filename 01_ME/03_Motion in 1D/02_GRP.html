Graphical Analysis of Motion

The visualisation of motion

Graphs are powerful tools for visualizing data and understanding relationships between variables. They're used in various fields, not just physics. For instance, a cricket match score graph shows how the runs scored change over time, and a stock market graph tracks changes in stock prices.

Graphs can show increasing trends, decreasing trends, or no trend at all. An increasing trend means the y-axis variable increases as the x-axis variable increases. A decreasing trend means the y-axis variable decreases as the x-axis variable increases. The slope of the graph represents this trend quantitatively.

Slope of a graph

The slope of a graph quantifies how steep the graph is. For a straight-line graph, the slope is constant. A steeper line has a higher magnitude of slope (it could be positive or negative), indicating a faster rate of change. A flatter line has a lower slope magnitude, showing a slower rate of change.

Imagine a graph of temperature throughout the day. From morning to noon, the temperature typically increases. This would be represented by an upward-sloping line on the graph, indicating a positive slope as shown:

The positive slope tells us that as time increases (moves to the right on the x-axis), the temperature also increases (moves up on the y-axis). As time progresses from noon to evening, the temperature generally decreases, resulting in a downward-sloping line with a negative slope. Here, as time increases, the temperature decreases.

For curves, every point may have different slope, in that case we draw tangent at desired point to find the slope at that point.

If we draw a line connecting two points A and B on a curved position-time graph, this line is called a secant, and its slope gives the average velocity between those two times.

In the above graph, the value of \(\tan\theta\) is known as slope of line \(AB\). Its slope is given as:

\(\qquad \text{Slope}=\tan\theta=\dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\)

Thus, slope of the secant \(AB\) represents nothing but the average velocity in the time interval of \(\Delta t\).

\(\Rightarrow\quad \text{Average velocity}=\tan\theta\)

As the time interval between points A and B gets smaller and smaller (the points get closer),

  • the secant line AB approaches the tangent to the curve at point B
  • the average velocity between AB approaches the instantaneous velovity at point B

The slope of a tangent drawn at a point in any general graph, represents the rate of change of quantity on \(y\)-axis with respect to \(x\)-axis.

Area of a graph

The total displacement becomes \(24 \,\text{m}\). Notice that each term in the sum is the area of a rectangular section under the velocity-time graph. This leads to a general principle:

The total displacement is the area under the velocity-time graph

If the velocity changes continuously (not in steps), we can still apply this principle. We divide the time into very small intervals \(\Delta t\). Over each small interval, we assume the velocity remains essentially constant. The displacement during each interval is approximately \(v \Delta t\) (the area of a very thin rectangle). Summing up these small displacements (or areas) gives an approximation of the total displacement.

As \(\Delta t\) becomes infinitesimally small, this sum approaches the exact area under the curve, which is the precise displacement. This is shown in animated graph below:

This process of summing infinitesimally small areas is known as integration in calculus. For finite size of the time intervals, the displacement will be:

\(\qquad \displaystyle\Delta s={{v}_{1}}\Delta t+{{v}_{2}}\Delta t+{{v}_{3}}\Delta t+{{v}_{4}}\Delta t+...+{{v}_{n}}\Delta t\)

\(\Rightarrow\quad\displaystyle \Delta s=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}{{{v}_{i}}\Delta t}\)

But if each time interval becomes infinitasimally small, we write the same summation in terms of integration as:

\(\qquad \displaystyle\Delta s=\int\limits_{{{t}_{1}}}^{{{t}_{2}}}{vdt}\)

Thus,

For any general graph of \(y\) vs. \(x\), the area below the curve is represented by the integration: \(\displaystyle\int\limits_{{{x}_{1}}}^{{{x}_{2}}}{ydx}\)

Before we proceed into some standard graphs generally used to understand motion of an object, lets conclude about the two direct informations we get from any graph:

  • The slope of graph represents rate of change of quantity on \(y\)-axis relative to the quantity on \(x\)-axis. This rate of change is simply the differentiation of quantity on \(y\)-axis with respect to the quantity on \(x\)-axis

  • The area under the curve in a graph shows how the quantity on the \(y\)-axis builds up as the quantity on the \(x\)-axis changes. Mathematically, this is the integral of \(y\) with respect to \(x\).

Using the concepts discussed so far, the following graphical relations between displacement \(s\), velocity \(v\) and the acceleration \(a\) vs. time \(t\) become obvious :

\(s-t\text{ graph}\xrightarrow{\mathbf{Differentiation}}v\)

\(v-t\text{ graph}\xrightarrow{\mathbf{Differentiation}}{a}\)

\(a-t\text{ graph}\xrightarrow{\mathbf{Integration}}\text{Change in }v\)

\(v-t\text{ graph}\xrightarrow{\mathbf{Integration}} \text{Change in position}(s)\)

Velocity-time graphs are particularly useful because they provide multiple pieces of information about an object's motion. The slope of the graph gives the instantaneous acceleration (\(a = \frac{dv}{dt}\)), and the area under the curve gives the displacement. Now, for different types of motion performed by an object the examples of graphs are enlisted as follows:

  • Case 1: Constant Velocity

    In this case, the slope of velocity time graph is zero, indicating zero acceleration. The displacement increases linearly with time.

  • Case 2: Increasing Velocity (Constant Acceleration)

    The slope of velocity time graph is positive and constant, indicating constant positive acceleration. The displacement increases with time at an increasing rate (curved graph).

  • Case 3: Decreasing Velocity (Constant Deceleration)

    The slope of velocity time graph is negative and constant, representing constant negative acceleration (deceleration). The displacement initially increases but at a decreasing rate, eventually reaching a maximum and potentially starting to decrease if the velocity becomes negative.

In the real world, motion is rarely limited to constant or stepwise slopes (like straight lines). Most motions involve curves, where the slope changes continuously. Understanding these curves is critical for analyzing the dynamics of motion.

A continuously changing slope indicates a variable rate of change. In a position-time graph, this means the velocity isn't constantâsuggesting acceleration or deceleration. Similarly, in a velocity-time graph, a changing slope indicates non-uniform acceleration.

Understanding Linearly Changing Slopes

One special case of continuously changing slopes is when the slope itself changes at a constant rate. In a position-time graph, this would create a parabolic curve, such as \( s = t^2 \). The corresponding slope (velocity) increases linearly, forming a straight line in the slope-time graph.

In the above graph, the slope (velocity) changes linearly over time. For instance:

  • The slope is negative at \(t = -2\), indicating backward motion.
  • The slope becomes zero at \(t = 0\), indicating the object is momentarily at rest.
  • The slope increases positively after \(t = 0\), reflecting forward motion with increasing speed.
This curve represents non-uniform motion.

Key Insights for Curved Graphs

When interpreting curved graphs, keep these points in mind:

  • Positive Slope: Indicates increasing position or velocity.
  • Negative Slope: Indicates decreasing position or velocity.
  • Steeper Curve: Represents a faster rate of change (greater velocity or acceleration).
  • Flatter Curve: Represents a slower rate of change (lower velocity or acceleration).
  • Inflection Points: Points where the curvature changes, indicating a change in acceleration's direction.
