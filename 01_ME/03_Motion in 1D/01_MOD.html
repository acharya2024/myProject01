Motion in a Straight Line

All about displacement, velocity and acceleration.

Distance is a measure of the actual path length traversed by an object between two points. It is important to note that distance is a property of the path taken, not just the initial and final positions. It also inherently involves a time interval over which the motion occurs.

Average speed is defined as the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

\( \qquad \text{Average Speed} = \dfrac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}} \)

For example, if a car travels a distance of 100 kilometers in 2 hours, its average speed is:

\( \qquad \text{Average Speed} = \dfrac{100 \, \text{km}}{2 \, \text{h}} = 50 \, \text{km/h} \)

This section focuses on problem-solving techniques related to average speed when the speed of an object is not constant.

  • Case 1: Total Distance and Total Time Given

    If the total distance and total time are given, the average speed is simply calculated as:

    \( \qquad \text{Average Speed} = \dfrac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}} \)

    Example: A cyclist travels a total distance of 50 km in 2.5 hours. The average speed is \(50 \, \text{km} / 2.5 \, \text{h} = 20 \, \text{km/h}\).

  • Case 2: Equal Time Intervals, Different Speeds

    If a particle travels for two equal time intervals \(t\) with speeds \(v_1\) and \(v_2\), covering distances \(d_1 = v_1 t\) and \(d_2 = v_2 t\) respectively, the average speed is:

    \( \qquad v_{avg} = \dfrac{d_1 + d_2}{2t} = \dfrac{v_1 t + v_2 t}{2t} = \dfrac{v_1 + v_2}{2} \)

    Example: A car travels at 40 km/h for 1 hour and then at 60 km/h for another hour. The average speed is \((40 + 60)/2 = 50 \, \text{km/h}\).

  • Case 3: Unequal Time Intervals, Different Speeds

    If a particle travels for time intervals \(t_1\) and \(t_2\) with speeds \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) respectively, the average speed is:

    \( \qquad v_{avg} = \dfrac{v_1 t_1 + v_2 t_2}{t_1 + t_2}\)

    Example: A train travels at 70 km/h for 2 hours and then at 50 km/h for 3 hours. The average speed is \((70 \cdot 2 + 50 \cdot 3)/(2+3) = 58 \, \text{km/h}\).

  • Case 4: Equal Distance Intervals, Different Speeds

    If a particle travels equal distances \(d\) with speeds \(v_1\) and \(v_2\), the time taken for each part is \(t_1 = d/v_1\) and \(t_2 = d/v_2\). The average speed is:

    \( \qquad v_{avg} = \dfrac{2d}{t_1 + t_2} = \dfrac{2d}{\dfrac{d}{v_1} + \dfrac{d}{v_2}} = \dfrac{2v_1 v_2}{v_1 + v_2} \)

    Example: A person walks 10 km at 4 km/h and then another 10 km at 6 km/h. The average speed is \((2 \cdot 4 \cdot 6) / (4 + 6) = 4.8 \, \text{km/h}\).

  • Case 5: Unequal Distance Intervals, Different Speeds

    If a particle travels distances \(d_1\) and \(d_2\) with speeds \(v_1\) and \(v_2\), the average speed is:

    \( \qquad v_{avg} = \dfrac{d_1 + d_2}{\dfrac{d_1}{v_1} + \dfrac{d_2}{v_2}} \)

    Example: A plane flies 3000 km at 600 km/h and then 2000 km at 500 km/h. The average speed is \((3000 + 2000) / (3000/600 + 2000/500) = 545.45 \, \text{km/h}\).

Consider a particle moving according to the equation \(x = t^2\), where \(x\) is the position in meters and \(t\) is the time in seconds. To better understand this motion, the following table illustrates the particle's position at specific times:

Time (\(t\)) [s] Position (\(x = t^2\)) [m]
0 0
1 1
2 4
3 9
4 16
5 25

  • Let's start by finding the average speed from \(t = 0\) to \(t = 4\):

    At \(t = 0\), the position is \(x = 0^2 = 0 \, \text{m}\).
    At \(t = 4\), the position is \(x = 4^2 = 16 \, \text{m}\).
    Average speed is calculated as:

    \(\text{Average Speed} = \dfrac{16 - 0}{4 - 0} = 4 \, \text{m/s}\)

  • Now, let's calculate the average speed from \(t = 2\) to \(t = 5\):

    At \(t = 2\), the position is \(x = 2^2 = 4 \, \text{m}\).
    At \(t = 5\), the position is \(x = 5^2 = 25 \, \text{m}\).
    Average speed is:

    \(\text{Average Speed} = \dfrac{25 - 4}{5 - 2} = 7 \, \text{m/s}\)

  • The speed at \(t = 2 \, \text{s}\) represents how fast the particle is moving at that instant. Imagine an observer opening their eyes for a very short moment exactly at \(t = 2 \, \text{s}\). The question is: what speed would this observer measure?

    At first, you might think the speed is zero because the particle moves such a tiny distance during the short time. However, while the distance is small, the time interval is also very small. The ratio of distance to time—speed—doesn't necessarily have to be small. To understand this better, let's analyze it mathematically.

    To determine the instantaneous speed at \(t = 2 \, \text{s}\), we calculate the average speed over progressively smaller intervals around \(t = 2 \, \text{s}\).

    • From \(t = 2.0 \, \text{s}\) to \(t = 2.1 \, \text{s}\):

      \(\qquad v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{2.1^2 - 2^2}{2.1 - 2} \, \text{m/s}\)

      \(\Rightarrow\quad v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{4.41 - 4}{0.1} = 4.1 \, \text{m/s}\)

    • From \(t = 2.00 \, \text{s}\) to \(t = 2.01 \, \text{s}\):

      \(\qquad v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{2.01^2 - 2^2}{2.01 - 2} \, \text{m/s}\)

      \(\Rightarrow\quad v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{4.0401 - 4}{0.01} = 4.01 \, \text{m/s}\)

    • To generalize, let's calculate the average speed from \(t = 2 \, \text{s}\) to \(t = (2 + h) \, \text{s}\), where \(h\) is a very small positive number:

      \(\qquad v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{(2 + h)^2 - 2^2}{h} \, \text{m/s}\)

      \(\Rightarrow\quad v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{4 + 4h + h^2 - 4}{h} = (4 + h) \, \text{m/s}\)

    Now, the question arises: how small can we set \(h\)? An obvious answer might be \(h = 0\), but this leads to problems:

    • If \(h = 0\), it implies the observer never opened their eyes, as the observation interval becomes zero. Without an observation interval, measuring speed becomes meaningless.

    • During the calculation:

      \(\qquad \dfrac{4 + 4h + h^2 - 4}{h} = (4 + h) \, \text{m/s}\),

      we canceled \(h\) from the numerator and denominator. If \(h = 0\), we can no longer perform this cancellation, making the calculation undefined.

    To resolve this, we let \(h\) approach zero, denoted as \(h \to 0\). This means \(h\) becomes infinitesimally close to zero but never exactly zero. By doing so, we ensure a non-zero observation interval while also allowing us to simplify the calculation.

    As \(h \to 0\), the average speed calculated as \((4 + h) \, \text{m/s}\) approaches \(4 \, \text{m/s}\), because \(h\) becomes negligible when added to 4.

    Thus, the instantaneous speed of the particle at \(t = 2 \, \text{s}\) is \(4 \, \text{m/s}\).

    This process demonstrates how instantaneous speed is the limit of average speed over infinitesimally small time intervals. We will explore this concept further in the next section.

    Now with a more formal approach, let us try to calculate speed at any general time \(t\).

    The speed at \(t\) represents how fast the particle is moving at that instant. To determine this, we calculate the average speed over a very small time interval \(\Delta t\) around \(t\).

    From \(t\) to \(t + \Delta t\):

    \( \qquad v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{(t+\Delta t)^2 - t^2}{(t + \Delta t) - t} \)

    \( \Rightarrow v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{t^2 + 2t\Delta t + (\Delta t)^2 - t^2}{\Delta t} \)

    \( \Rightarrow v_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{2t\Delta t + (\Delta t)^2}{\Delta t} = 2t + \Delta t\)

    As \(\Delta t\) approaches zero (written as \(\Delta t \to 0\)), \(v_{\text{avg}}\) approaches \(2t\). Therefore, the instantaneous speed \(v\) at any time \(t\) is \(2t\).

    This means that the rate of change of \(t^2\) with respect to \(t\) is \(2t\). In calculus, we say that the derivative of \(t^2\) is \(2t\), and we represent this as:

    \( \qquad \dfrac{d}{dt}(t^2) = 2t \)

    This concept of finding the rate of change at any instant is fundamental to calculus, and we'll explore it further in the next section with more general functions.

Instantaneous speed represents how fast an object is moving at a specific instant in time. It's like taking the concept of average speed and shrinking the time interval until it becomes infinitesimally small. This allows us to measure the rate of change of position at an exact moment.

Mathematically, if \(x\) represents the position of an object at time \(t\), the instantaneous speed \(v\) is defined as:

\(\qquad v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \dfrac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{dx}{dt} \)

This formula introduces the concept of differentiation, represented by \(\dfrac{dx}{dt}\), which means "the rate of change of position (\(x\)) with respect to time (\(t\))." It tells us how much the position changes for an infinitesimally small change in time.

Differentiation allows us to calculate instantaneous rates of change, such as speed. It's a powerful tool in physics and mathematics. For example, in the function \(x = t^2\), the derivative tells us how \(x\) changes with time. The mathematical result for its derivative is written as:

\(\qquad \dfrac{dx}{dt}=\dfrac{t^2}{t}=2t\)

To build familiarity, here's a table of basic differentiation formulas. These will help us compute instantaneous speeds for various types of motion.

Function, \(x\) Derivative, \(\dfrac{dx}{dt}\)
\(t^2\) \(2t\)
\(t^3\) \(3t^2\)
\(t^n\) \(nt^{n-1}\)
\(\sqrt{t}\) \(\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{t}}\)
\(t^2+t^3\) \(2t+3t^2\)
Let's Apply These Formulas

Understanding differentiation is easiest when we apply it to specific examples. Let's calculate the instantaneous speed for different types of motion. FOllowing examples contain different expressions of distance \(x\) in meters depending on \(t\) in seconds. Lets find dependence of speed \(v\) on time in each case.

  • Example 1: \(x = 5t^3\)

    Here, \(x = 5t^3\). To find \(v\), we differentiate \(x\) with respect to \(t\):

    \(\qquad v = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 5 \cdot 3t^{3-1} = 15t^2\)

    This tells us that the instantaneous speed at any time \(t\) is \(15t^2\). For instance:

    • At \(t = 1 \, \text{s}\), \(v = 15 \cdot 1^2 = 15 \, \text{m/s}\)
    • At \(t = 2 \, \text{s}\), \(v = 15 \cdot 2^2 = 60 \, \text{m/s}\)
  • Example 2: \(x = 7t + 3\)

    For this linear motion, \(x = 7t + 3\). Differentiating:

    \(\qquad v = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 7\)

    Here, the instantaneous speed is constant and equals \(7 \, \text{m/s}\), regardless of the time \(t\).

  • Example 3: \(x = 2t^2 + 4t + 1\)

    For this quadratic motion, \(x = 2t^2 + 4t + 1\). Differentiating:

    \(\qquad v = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 2 \cdot 2t + 4 = 4t + 4\)

    This gives the instantaneous speed as a function of time. For instance:

    • At \(t = 1 \, \text{s}\), \(v = 4 \cdot 1 + 4 = 8 \, \text{m/s}\)
    • At \(t = 3 \, \text{s}\), \(v = 4 \cdot 3 + 4 = 16 \, \text{m/s}\)

Displacement, unlike distance, is a vector quantity. It measures the change in position of an object, considering both the magnitude and direction of the change. It depends only on the initial and final positions, not the path taken.

In one dimension, displacement can be represented by positive and negative values along a straight line, for example, the x-axis. A positive displacement means movement in the positive x-direction (e.g., east), and a negative displacement indicates movement in the negative x-direction (e.g., west).

For example, if a person walks 10 m east and then 3 m west, the total distance traveled is 10 m + 3 m = 13 m. However, the displacement is +10 m - 3 m = +7 m (or 7 m east).

Average Velocity

Average velocity is defined as the displacement covered by a particle in a given time interval divided by the time interval:

\( \qquad \text{Average Velocity} = \dfrac{\text{Displacement}} {\text{Time Interval}}\)

For example, consider a particle that starts at \(x = 0\), moves to \(x = +5\) m in 2 seconds, and then to \(x = +2\) m in another 3 seconds. Lets find its average velocity in following steps:

\(\qquad\)Displacement (0 to 2s) = \(+5\) m.

\(\qquad\)Displacement (2s to 5s) = \(+2\) m \(-\) \(+5\) m = \(-3\) m.

\(\qquad\)Total Displacement (0 to 5s) = \(+2\) m.

\(\qquad\)Now, average velocity (0 to 5s) = \(\dfrac{+2 \, \text{m}}{5 \, \text{s}} = +0.4\) m/s.

Instantaneous Velocity

Instantaneous velocity, similar to instantaneous speed, is the velocity at a specific moment. Instantaneous velocity at a time \(t\) is the average velocity from time \(t\) to time \(t+\Delta t\) such that the time interval \(\Delta t\) is infinitesimally small (\(\Delta t\to0\)). It's the rate of change of displacement with respect to time. We calculate it using differentiation:

\( \qquad v = \dfrac{dx}{dt}\)

The difference between instantaneous speed and velocity is that velocity includes direction (positive or negative in one dimension). A negative velocity means the object is moving in the negative x-direction.

For a given position equation x(t) lets calculate instantaneous velocity using following examples with position \(x(t)\) in meters depending on time \(t\) in seconds as:

Example 1: \(x(t) = 2t^2 - 3t\)

Differentiating x(t) with respect to time:

\(\qquad v(t) = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 4t - 3\)

This gives us the instantaneous velocity at any time \(t\).

  • At \(t=1\)s, \(v(1) = 4(1) - 3 = +1\) m/s (moving in positive direction).
  • At \(t=2\)s, \(v(2) = 4(2) - 3 = +5\) m/s (moving in positive direction).
  • At \(t=3\)s, \(v(3) = 4(3) - 3 = +9\) m/s (moving in positive direction).
Example 2: \(x(t) = t^3 + 2\)

Differentiating:

\(\qquad v(t) = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 3t^2\)

  • At \(t=0.5\)s, \(v(0.5) = 3(0.5)^2 = +0.75\) m/s.
  • At \(t=1\)s, \(v(1) = 3(1)^2 = +3\) m/s.
  • At \(t=2\)s, \(v(2) = 3(2)^2 = +12\) m/s.
Example 3: \(x(t) = 4t\)

Differentiating:

\(\qquad v(t) = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = 4\)

The velocity is constant, \(v(t) = +4\) m/s for all \(t\).

Example 4: \(x(t) = 5 - 2t^2\)

Differentiating:

\(\qquad v(t) = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = -4t\)

  • At \(t=1\)s, \(v(1) = -4(1) = -4\) m/s (moving in negative direction).
  • At \(t=2\)s, \(v(2) = -4(2) = -8\) m/s (moving in negative direction).
Example 5: \(x(t) = \dfrac{1}{2}at^2 + ut + x_0\) (where \(a\), \(u\), and \(x_0\) are constants)

This is a general equation for motion with constant acceleration.

Differentiating:

\(\qquad v(t) = \dfrac{dx}{dt} = at + u\)

The velocity is a linear function of time. If \(a=2 \, \text{m/s}^2\), \(u=5 \, \text{m/s}\), and \(x_0=1 \, \text{m}\), then:

  • At \(t=0\)s, \(v(0) = 2(0) + 5 = +5\) m/s.
  • At \(t=3\)s, \(v(3) = 2(3) + 5 = +11\) m/s.

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. It's a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

First, average acceleration is the change in velocity in a given time interval divided by the time interval over which the change occurs:

\( \qquad a_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{v_f - v_i}{t_f - t_i}\)

where \(v_i\) and \(v_f\) are the initial and final velocities at times \(t_i\) and \(t_f\) respectively.

For example, if a car's velocity changes from \(+20 \, \text{m/s}\) to \(+30 \, \text{m/s}\) in \(5 \, \text{s}\), then its average acceleration is:

\(\qquad a_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{30 - 20}{5} = 2 \, \text{m/s}^2\)

If the velocity changes from \(+10 \, \text{m/s}\) to \(-5 \, \text{m/s}\) in 2 seconds, then

\(\qquad a_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{-5 - 10}{2} = -7.5 \, \text{m/s}^2\)

The negative sign signifies that the acceleration is in the opposite direction to the initial velocity (deceleration).

Instantaneous Acceleration

Instantaneous acceleration is the acceleration at a specific instant in time. It's the limit of the average acceleration as the time interval approaches zero. Just as instantaneous velocity is the derivative of position with respect to time, instantaneous acceleration is the derivative of velocity with respect to time:

\( \qquad a = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{dv}{dt} \)

For example, if the velocity \(v(t)\) in m/s of a particle depends on time \(t\) in seconds given by \(v(t) = 3t^2 - 2t + 1\), its instantaneous acceleration is:

\(\qquad a(t) = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = 6t - 2\)

  • At \(t=1\)s, \(a(1) = 6(1) - 2 = 4\) m/s².
  • At \(t=2\)s, \(a(2) = 6(2) - 2 = 10\) m/s².

Uniformly Accelerated Motion

Uniformly accelerated motion means that the acceleration of an object remains constant over time—it doesn't change in magnitude or direction. In such cases, we can use a set of equations to describe the motion. These are known as the equations of motion.

First Equation of Motion

If acceleration \(a\) is constant, the change in velocity \(\Delta v\) over a time interval \(\Delta t = t - 0 = t\) is simply \(a \times t \). If the initial velocity is u, at time t=0, and the final velocity is \(v\), then \(\Delta v = v - u\).

\(\qquad a \cdot t = v-u\)

\(\qquad \Rightarrow v = u + at \)

Example 1:

A car starts from rest (\(u=0\)) and accelerates at \(2 \, \text{m/s}^2\) for \(5 \, \text{s}\). Its final velocity is:

\(\qquad v = 0 + (2)(5) = 10 \, \text{m/s}\)

Example 2:

A train moving at \(20 \, \text{m/s}\) decelerates at \(1 \, \text{m/s}^2\) for \(10 \, \text{s}\). Its final velocity is:

\(\qquad v = 20 + (-1)(10) = 10 \, \text{m/s}\)

Second Equation of Motion

For uniformly accelerated motion, the average velocity is given by:

\( \qquad v_{\text{avg}} = \frac{u + v}{2} \)

The displacement \(s\) is the average velocity multiplied by the time \(t\):

\( \qquad s = v_{\text{avg}} \cdot t = \left(\frac{u+v}{2}\right) t\)

Substituting \(v = u + at\) from the first equation:

\( \qquad s = \left(\frac{u + u + at}{2}\right) t = \left(\frac{2u+at}{2}\right)t\)

\( \qquad \Rightarrow s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \)

Example 1:

A ball is thrown upward with an initial velocity of \(10 \, \text{m/s}\). How high does it go before stopping momentarily? (Assume \(a = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\))

When it stops, \(v=0\). Using the third equation (derived next):

\( \qquad v^2=u^2+2as\)

\(\qquad 0=10^2+2(-9.8)(s)\)

\(\qquad 19.6 s=100\)

\(\qquad \Rightarrow s=5.1 m\)

Example 2:

A car initially at rest accelerates at \(5 \, \text{m/s}^2\) for \(10 \, \text{s}\). The distance it travels is:

\(\qquad s = (0)(10) + \frac{1}{2}(5)(10^2) = 250 \, \text{m}\)

Example 3:

A stone is dropped from a cliff and hits the ground below after \(4 \, \text{s}\). What is the height of the cliff? (Assume \(a = +9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\))

\(s=(0)(4)+1/2(9.8)(16)= 78.4 \text{m}\)

Third Equation of Motion

From the first equation, \(t = (v - u) / a\). Substituting this into the second equation:

\( \qquad s = u\left(\frac{v-u}{a}\right) + \frac{1}{2}a\left(\frac{v-u}{a}\right)^2\)

\( \qquad \Rightarrow 2as= 2uv -2u^2 + v^2 -2uv + u^2\)

\( \qquad \Rightarrow v^2 = u^2 + 2as \)

Example 1:

A car moving at \(20 \, \text{m/s}\) brakes with a constant deceleration of \(5 \, \text{m/s}^2\). How far does it travel before stopping?

\(\qquad 0 = 20^2 + 2(-5)s \Rightarrow s = 40 \, \text{m}\)

Example 2:

A ball is dropped from a height of \(10 \, \text{m}\). What is its speed just before hitting the ground? (Take \(a = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\))

\(\qquad v^2 = 0 + 2(9.8)(10) \Rightarrow v \approx 14 \, \text{m/s}\)

Example 3:

A cyclist accelerates uniformly from \(10 \, \text{m/s}\) to \(20 \, \text{m/s}\) covering \(30 \, \text{m}\). What is his acceleration?

\(20^2=10^2+2(a)(30)\)

\(60a=400-100\)

\(a=300/60=5 \text{m/s}^2\)

Summary Table for Equations of Motion

Equation When to Use
\(v = u + at\) When displacement (\(s\)) is not given or required.
\(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\) When final velocity (\(v\)) is not given or required.
\(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\) When time (\(t\)) is not given or required.

Important Note: These equations are valid only for uniformly accelerated motion.
