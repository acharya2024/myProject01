Consider a body kept in stable equilibrium, if this body is displaced slightly from its equilibrium position, it will continue to move back and forth around its equilibrium position. We will study all such types of motion that repeat themselves in regular time interval under following categories:

  • Periodic motionPeriodic motion of a body is that motion which is repeated exactly after a fixed interval of time. Mathematically, if a function of time \(f\left(t\right)\) can be expressed as \(f\left(t+T\right)\)=\(f\left(t\right)\), then the function can be regarded as periodic with period \(T\). Motion of any particle described by such periodic function will be a periodic motion . Following are a few examples of periodic motion:

    1. The revolution of earth around the sun is a periodic motion. Its period of revolution is one year
    2. The motion of hands of a clock is a periodic motion. The period of motion of hour’s hand of a clock is 12 hours, of minute's hand of a clock is 1 hour and of second’s hand of a clock is one minute.
    3. Uniform circular motion is a periodic motion.
    4. A pendulum performs periodic motion, as it repeats its motion after a regular time interval.

Thus every oscillatory motion is a periodic motion but only that periodic motion which is bounded between two extremes are called oscillatory motion.   • Simple harmonic motion

    Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a type of periodic motion that is commonly encountered in many different physical systems. It is a particularly important concept in the study of physics, as it is the basis for understanding many other types of oscillatory behavior, such as sound waves and electromagnetic waves.

    If a particle is placed under equilibrium at a position, net force acting on the particle is zero at that position. Consider a situation in which, if the particle gets displaced from this equilibrium position it experiences a net force that moves it back to previous position.

    The restoring force always tending to restore particle to its equilibrium position is shown by arrow in following animation:

    Such force tending to restore the particle to equilibrium position is called restoring force and this equilibrium position is called mean position. If such a restoring force becomes linearly dependent on displacement of particle, the resulting motion of particle becomes simple harmonic motion.

    What type of force do you think is the restoring force? Constant force Variable force

    In exact words, a particle performing simple harmonic motion oscillates in straight line in such a way that the acceleration \(\vec{a}\) is proportional to negative of displacement vector \(\vec{x}\) from mean position. Thus requirement for a particle to perform simple harmonic motion is

    \(\qquad\vec{a}\propto -\vec{x}\)

    \(\qquad\vec{a} = -\omega^2\vec{x}\)

    where \(\vec{x}\) is the displacement of the particle measured from mean position and \(\omega\) represents a constant. The square of the term \(\omega\) ensures that the proportionality constant remains positive. The acceleration of particle is always along direction opposite to direction of displacement of the particle. We can conclude that acceleration of a particle executing simple harmonic motion is always directed towards its mean position as shown:

    A particle in SHM is in equilibrium at its mean position. What type of equilibrium is this equilibrium? Stable equilibrium Unstable equilibrium Neutral equilibrium

    If a particle performs simple harmonic motion, its motion is usually analysed by measuring displacement from mean position. For a particle in SHM, the maximum magnitude of displacement of the particle from mean position is called amplitude. The time taken to complete one cycle of the motion is called time period of the SHM.

We observe that every such kind of SHM of particle has some mean position where the particle is in equilibrium and if the particle gets displaced from mean position, net force acts on the particle to restore the particle to its equilibrium. Such resultant force acting on the particle which always acts towards the mean position is called the restoring force as described previously.

SHM is also observed in waves, such as water waves, sound waves, and electromagnetic waves. In all these cases, the particles that make up the wave oscillate back and forth around their equilibrium positions.

SHM has many practical applications in everyday life. For example, the motion of a car's suspension system as it drives over bumps and potholes can be modeled as simple harmonic motion. The vibration of a guitar string and the oscillation of a tuning fork are also examples of simple harmonic motion. SHM is also used in the design of various mechanical and electrical systems, such as clocks, watches, and even earthquake-resistant buildings.

In summary, simple harmonic motion is a type of periodic motion that occurs when an object oscillates back and forth around an equilibrium position under the influence of a restoring force that is proportional to its displacement. It is a fundamental concept in physics and has many practical applications in daily life.

    The previous relation between acceleration \(\vec{a}\) and the displacement \(\vec{x}\) can be rewritten as:

    \(\qquad x=A\sin\left(\omega t + \phi\right)\)

    From the relation of position and time, we can use differentiation to get relation between velocity and time. Thus we have:

    \(\qquad x=A\sin\left(\omega t + \phi\right)\)      ...(i)

In dynamics of SHM, we study the forces acting in SHM and their relation with time period and frequency of SHM. For a particle to perform SHM about a mean position, there must be a net force acting on the particle when it gets displaced from its mean position. Such force is known as restoring force. In SHM, restoring force causes the particle to again reach its mean position. Hence the restoring force is always directed towards mean position.

Consider a particle of mass \(m\) performing SHM. The force \(\vec{F}\) depends on displacement from mean position \(\vec{x}\) as:

\(\qquad \vec{F}=-k \,\vec{x}\)[with \(k\) as a propotionality constant]

Since this force is always directed towards the mean position, this force can be considered as a restoring force for the system. A similar equation is followed by any system undergoing SHM.

Using Newton's second law for the particle,

\(\qquad m\,\vec{a}=-k \,\vec{x}\)[with \(\vec{a}\) as a acceleration]

\( \Rightarrow\quad\vec{a}=-\frac{k}{m} \,\vec{x}\)

This equation represents an equation of SHM. With comparison to previously developed equation in definition of SHM,

\(\qquad \omega^2=\frac{k}{m}\)[with \(\omega\) as a angular frequency]

\(\Rightarrow\quad \omega=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}\)

\(\Rightarrow\quad \frac{2\pi}{T}=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}\)

\(\Rightarrow\quad T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}\)

For any particle of mass \(m\) in SHM, if proportionality constant of restoring force with negative of displacement from mean position is \(k\), time period of SHM will be \(2\pi\sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}\). It can be observed that if the inertia of system increases, time period for motion increases while if the restoring force becomes strong then the time period decreases.

Since the particle in SHM is in motion, the particle has kinetic energy. If conservative forces act on a particle undergoing SHM, potential energy is associated with the motion. These two types of energies, along with their sum : mechanical energy, are discussed in following sections:

  • Kinetic energy

    The kinetic energy of the particle varies as the particle changes its speed during the motion. The particle has maximum kinetic energy at the mean position and zero kinetic energy at the extreme positions. At any general position, where particle of mass \(m\) has velocity \(\vec{v}\), its kinetic energy is given as:

    \(\qquad\text{K.E.}=\frac{1}{2}mv^2\)

    Using previously developed result for velocity,

    \(\qquad\text{K.E.}=\frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 \left(A^2-x^2\right)\) [Using \(v=\omega\sqrt{A^2-x^2}\)]

    Expressed in terms of time,

    \(\qquad\text{K.E.}=\frac{1}{2}mA^2\omega^2\cos^2\left(\omega t+\phi\right)\) [Using \(v=A\omega\cos\left(\omega t+\phi\right)\)]

    Such variations of kinetic energy on position and time is shown in following animation:

  • Potential energy

    Assuming only conservative force \(\vec{F}\) to act on particle in SHM having displacement from mean position as \(\vec{x}\),

    \(\qquad\vec{F}=-k\vec{x}\)

    The equation can be correlated to force applied by spring which gives result for potential energy as:

    \(\qquad\text{P.E.}=\frac{1}{2}kx^2\)

    And in terms of time,

    \(\qquad\text{P.E.}=\frac{1}{2}k A^2\sin^2\left(\omega t+\phi\right)\) [Using \(x=A\sin\left(\omega t+\phi\right)\)]

    Such variations of potential energy on position and time is shown in following animation:

  • Mechanical energy

    The total mechanical energy of the system is the sum of the kinetic and potential energy. Therefore,

    \(\qquad\text{M.E.}=\text{K.E.}+\text{P.E.}\)

    \(\Rightarrow\quad\text{M.E.}=\frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 \left(A^2-x^2\right)+\frac{1}{2}kx^2\)

    \(\Rightarrow\quad\text{M.E.}=\frac{1}{2}k \left(A^2-x^2\right)+\frac{1}{2}kx^2\) [Using \(\omega^2=\frac{k}{m}\)]

    \(\Rightarrow\quad\text{M.E.}=\frac{1}{2}k \left(A^2\right)\) [Using \(\omega^2=\frac{k}{m}\)]

    This result for mechanical energy shows that the mechanical energy of systems in simple harmonic motion under conservative forces, remains constant with variation in position of particle or passage of time. Thus, mechanical energy of systems in SHM will be conserved provided only conservative forces act on the system.

If a system is already in SHM due to a restoring force, other forces may cause the SHM to change its mean position, frequency or amplitude. Such motion of system, when forces other than restoring force also act on the system, is called forced oscillation. Based on nature of extra applied forces \(\vec{F}_\text{applied}\) on the SHM, various effects on SHM are described below:

  • If \(F_\text{applied}\) is constant

    Consider a SHM in which particle moves around its equilibrium position \(P\) under the action of restoring force \(\vec{F}_\text{restoring}\). If a constant force \(\vec{F}_\text{applied}\) starts acting on the particle at all times, net force will become non-zero at point \(P\) and point \(P\) will no longer remain an equilibrium position. The equilibrium will now shift to another point \(P^\prime\) where the applied force exactly cancels the restoring force. Thus,

    At \(P\) :\(\qquad \vec{F}_\text{restoring}=0\)

    At \(P^\prime\) :\(\qquad \vec{F}_\text{restoring}+\vec{F}_\text{applied}=0\)

    The particle will still perform SHM but about point \(P^\prime\) with a force \(\vec{F}_\text{restoring}+\vec{F}_\text{applied}\) as an equivalent restoring force.

  • If \(F_\text{applied}\) always opposes velocity

    In SHM, the total mechanical energy remains constant when all forces are conservative. If a force opposes the particle's velocity, it performs net negative work during a complete cycle, causing the energy of the system to decrease. This force is called damping force, and the phenomenon is called damped oscillation. The restoring force still drives the particle to the equilibrium position, but the damping force removes energy from the system, and the amplitude of the oscillations gradually decreases until the particle comes to rest.

    The net force acting on the particle under SHM becomes \(\vec{F}_\text{restoring}+\vec{F}_\text{damping}\)

    If particle of mass \(m\) is under SHM of with restoring force of \(-kx\), with \(x\) as displacement of particle from mean position, equation of motion of particle can be mentioned as

    \(\qquad m \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-kx\)

    which has a solution of the form:

    \(\qquad x=A\sin\left( \omega t +\phi\right)\)[with sysmbols having usual meaning as earlier]

    But if, in addition to restoring force \(-kx\), a damping force proportional to negative of velocity \(-bv\) acts on particle then equation of motion becomes:

    \(\qquad m \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}=-kx - b \frac{dx}{dt}\)

    Here, \(b\) is the damping coefficient. Such differential equation has the following solution:

    \(\qquad x = A_\text{eq}\sin(\omega_\text{eq} + \phi)\)

    where \(A_\text{eq}\) represents equivalent amplitude of the resulting motion while \omega_\text{eq} is the equivalent angular frequency. Their values are given as:

    \(\qquad A_\text{eq}=Ae^{-\frac{b}{2m}t}\) and

    \(\qquad \omega_\text{eq}=\sqrt{\frac{k}{m}-\frac{b^2}{4m^2}}\)

    It can be observed that the equivalent amplitude of the motion decrease exponentially with time as the particle loses energy due to the damping force. For example, consider a mass attached to a spring. If you push the mass and then let go, it will oscillate back and forth due to the spring force. However, if you place the mass in a viscous fluid, such as honey, the motion will be damped, and the amplitude and frequency of the motion will decrease until the mass comes to rest.

    Damped oscillation is a common phenomenon encountered in our daily lives. A swing is an excellent example of a system that undergoes simple harmonic motion when pushed by a small amount. The swing moves back and forth in a regular manner with a constant period and amplitude. However, air resistance acting as a damping force against the swing's motion eventually brings it to rest. The damping force removes energy from the system, causing the amplitude of the oscillations to decrease gradually until the swing comes to rest.

    In summary, damping force acts as an opposing force against the motion of the system, resulting in the dissipation of energy from the system. Consequently, the amplitude of the oscillations decreases with time, and the system ultimately comes to rest.
