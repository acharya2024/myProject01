Gauss's Law

The law of field lines

In physics, the area associated with a surface is treated as a vector quantity. For a small, flat surface \( S \), its area vector is defined as the vector perpendicular (normal) to the surface, with a magnitude equal to the area of \( S \). Each surface possesses an area vector that is perpendicular to it, and the magnitude of this vector corresponds to the surface area. By convention, the direction of the area vector points outward, perpendicular to the surface. Specifically, for closed surfaces such as spheres or cuboids, the area vectors consistently point outward, representing the outward-facing normals.

Additionally, area vectors can be decomposed or resolved into their respective components, similar to other vector quantities like forces. If the area vector associated with a surface is \(\vec{A}\), it can be expressed in terms of its components \(A_x\), \(A_y\), and \(A_z\) along the three coordinate axes as follows:

\(\qquad \vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j} + A_z\hat{k}\)

The component \(A_x\) corresponds to a surface lying in the \(y\)-\(z\) plane. Similarly, the components \(A_y\) and \(A_z\) are associated with surfaces in the \(x\)-\(z\) plane and the \(x\)-\(y\) plane, respectively. This decomposition is illustrated in the following diagram:

On curved surfaces, the overall area vector can be considered as the vector sum of numerous infinitesimal area vectors \(\mathrm{d}\vec{S}\). For instance, take a curved section of a sphere. By summing all the tiny outward-pointing area vectors that constitute this section, we obtain the total vector area for that segment. Notably, for a closed surface, the total (net) area vector is always zero because the outward area vectors on opposite sides cancel each other out. This cancellation is a fundamental geometric principle underlying Gauss’s law.

The concept of a solid angle is the three-dimensional analogue of a plane angle in two dimensions. It measures how “large” an object appears when viewed from a particular point. Formally, the solid angle subtended by an object at a point is the area it projects on a unit sphere centered at that point. Its SI unit is the steradian (sr). A full sphere subtends a solid angle of \(4\pi \, \mathrm{sr}\). As a simple example, if you stand on flat ground and look upward, the open sky above you subtends about \(2\pi \, \mathrm{sr}\), corresponding to half of the total sphere: [Diagram showing the relationship between a sphere and solid angle].

A useful property of solid angles is that two surfaces, sharing the same boundary, will subtend the same solid angle at a given point, whether one surface is curved and the other is flat. To see why, imagine cutting off a “cap” from a sphere. The curved spherical cap and any flat surface that “seals” that cap along the same boundary curve subtend the same solid angle at the sphere’s center. This principle can be applied to simplify many geometric and physical calculations.

If a spherical cap subtends a semi-apex angle \(\theta\) at the center of the sphere, its solid angle \(\Omega\) is given by:

\(\Omega = 2\pi (1 - \cos \theta)\)

This expression connects the geometry of the cap (the angle \(\theta\)) to the size of the solid angle. In electromagnetism and other areas of physics, solid angles often come into play when calculating flux through spherical surfaces or understanding radiation patterns from a point source. Recognizing how much of a sphere’s surface is “covered” by an object or field lines is essential to accurate computations of flux and related quantities.
