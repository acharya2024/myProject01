The law of field lines

In physics, the area of a surface is often regarded as a vector quantity. For a small, flat surface \(S\), its area vector is defined as the vector perpendicular (normal) to the surface, having a magnitude equal to the area of \(S\). By this convention, all area vectors for closed surfaces (such as those on spheres or cuboids) point outward, consistent with outward-facing normals.

The 3D diagrams below help visualize this concept: each face or patch of a shape has an area vector that points away from the enclosed region.

Just like any other vector, area vectors can be broken down into components. If the area vector for a surface is \(\vec{A}\), it can be written as:

\(\quad \vec{A} = A_x \hat{i} \;+\; A_y \hat{j} \;+\; A_z \hat{k}\).

Here, \(A_x\) is the portion of the area vector pointing along the \(x\)-axis (i.e., corresponding to the surface’s projection in the \(y\)-\(z\) plane), \(A_y\) along the \(y\)-axis (projection in the \(x\)-\(z\) plane), and \(A_z\) along the \(z\)-axis (projection in the \(x\)-\(y\) plane). To see how these components can be computed, imagine a rectangular surface of length \(l\) and breadth \(b\) that is inclined at some angle \(\theta\) from the horizontal. If you look at this rectangular patch from different views, you can identify the horizontal and vertical components of its area by projecting the surface onto those planes.

The area of the inclined surface in the diagram above is \( l \times b \). This surface is inclined at an angle \(\theta\) with respect to the horizontal plane. As a result, the projection of the inclined surface onto the horizontal plane forms an area of \( l b \cos\theta \), while the projection onto the vertical plane forms an area of \( l b \sin\theta \). This approach is consistent with the method of resolving any vector, such as force or displacement, into two perpendicular components.

These ideas extend naturally to curved surfaces. For a curved surface, the total area vector \(\vec{S}\) is the sum (or integral) of all the infinitesimal area vectors \(\mathrm{d}\vec{S}\). Each \(\mathrm{d}\vec{S}\) corresponds to a small patch on the curved surface, oriented perpendicularly outward. By integrating over the entire surface—like a spherical cap—the contributions from these small patches combine to form one resultant area vector. Notice that if the shape is symmetric about a vertical axis (like a dome), the vertical components of the infinitesimal area vectors add up along that axis. Consequently, when calculating the total, you can consider only the projections of these small patches onto the horizontal plane. All these concepts—area vectors, their components, and the summation of infinitesimal contributions—are critical building blocks for understanding flux. They set the stage for applying Gauss’s law, where the flux of certain fields (like the electric field) through a surface depends on precisely these ideas of area and its orientation.

An especially important result occurs for closed surfaces: the total area vector for a completely enclosed volume is always zero because all outward vectors on one part of the surface cancel with those on the opposite side. However, for an open surface bounded by a loop (like the edge of a circular rim), the net area vector depends only on that boundary. As a result, if a curved surface and a flat disc share the exact same boundary, they will have the same total area vector. Whether one surface is curved or flat, if they share the same boundary, the resultant (net) area vector is identical, emphasizing that the boundary alone determines the overall area vector for an open surface.

The concept of a solid angle is the three-dimensional analogue of a plane angle in two dimensions. It measures how “large” an object appears when viewed from a particular point. Formally, the solid angle subtended by an object at a point is the area it projects on a unit sphere centered at that point. Its SI unit is the steradian (sr). A full sphere subtends a solid angle of \(4\pi \, \mathrm{sr}\). As a simple example, if you stand on flat ground and look upward, the open sky above you subtends about \(2\pi \, \mathrm{sr}\), corresponding to half of the total sphere: [Diagram showing the relationship between a sphere and solid angle].

A useful property of solid angles is that two surfaces, sharing the same boundary, will subtend the same solid angle at a given point, whether one surface is curved and the other is flat. To see why, imagine cutting off a “cap” from a sphere. The curved spherical cap and any flat surface that “seals” that cap along the same boundary curve subtend the same solid angle at the sphere’s center. This principle can be applied to simplify many geometric and physical calculations.

If a spherical cap subtends a semi-apex angle \(\theta\) at the center of the sphere, its solid angle \(\Omega\) is given by:

\(\Omega = 2\pi (1 - \cos \theta)\)

This expression connects the geometry of the cap (the angle \(\theta\)) to the size of the solid angle. In electromagnetism and other areas of physics, solid angles often come into play when calculating flux through spherical surfaces or understanding radiation patterns from a point source. Recognizing how much of a sphere’s surface is “covered” by an object or field lines is essential to accurate computations of flux and related quantities.
