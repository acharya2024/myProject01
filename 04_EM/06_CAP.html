Capacitance

Conductor's capacity to hold charge

Capacitance is a measure of a conductor's ability to store charge. So, when we refer to capacitance of a conductors, we call it capacitor. An isolated capacitor is a single conductor, or a system of conductors, in isolation from external influences.

A capacitor can store energy in the form of potential energy in an electric field. In this chapter we'll discuss the capacity of conductors to hold charge and energy.

When a conductor is charged, its potential increases. It is found that for an isolated conductor potential \(V\) of the conductor is proportional to charge \(Q\) given to it.

\(\qquad V\propto Q\)

\(\Rightarrow\quad \dfrac{Q}{V}=\text{Constant}\)

The capacitance \(C\) of an isolated conductor is defined as the ratio of the charge \( Q \) stored on the conductor to the potential \( V \) of the conductor:

\(\qquad C = \dfrac{Q}{V} \)

In other words, capacitance of an isolated conductor can be defined as charge required to increase potential of the conductor by unit volt.

For an isolated conductor, the potential is determined relative to a reference point, often taken as infinity where the potential is assumed to be zero. Conductor should be of finite dimension, so that potential of infinity can be assumed to be zero.

For example, for an isolated spherical conductor of radius \( R \) containing some charge \(Q\) at potential \(V\), the capacitance can be derived from the formula:

\(\qquad V = \dfrac{Q}{4\pi \epsilon_0 R }\)

\(\Rightarrow\quad C_\text{Spherical condcutor} = 4\pi \epsilon_0 R \)

This result shows that the capacitance of a spherical conductor is directly proportional to its radius and is independent of the charge or potential.

    Following are some general properties of capacitance of a conductor:

  • Capacitance is a scalar quantity and its unit is Farad. The value of 1 Farad is:

    \(\qquad 1 \text{Farad} = \frac{\text{1}\,\,\text{Coulomb}}{\text{1}\,\,\text{Volt}}\)

    The dimensional formula of capacitance is \(\text{M}^{-1}\text{L}^{-2}\text{A}^{2}\text{T}^{4}\).

  • Capacitance of an isolated conductor depends on:

    • Shape and size of the conductor: On increasing the size, capacitance increases.
    • On surrounding medium: With increase in dielectric constant K, capacitance increases.
    • Presence of other conductors: When a neutral conductor is placed near a charged conductor capacitance of conductors increases.

  • Capacitance of a conductor does not depend on:

    • Charge on the conductor
    • Potential of the conductor
    • Potential energy of the conductor.

Energy Stored in Isolated Conductors

Work Done in Charging an Isolated Conductor

Work is to be done by external agent in charging the conductor. This work by external agent gets stored in form of potential energy. This total energy stored in electric field of conductor is called self-energy of conductor. To find total work done by external agent, we will consider the total charge Q on the conductor is brought by external agent one by one in steps of dq and the process occurs very slowly. This is shown in following diagram:

If a conductor has acquired a charge q, then work done by external agent in bringing further charge $dq$ from infinity on the conductor is: \[dW=\frac{q}{C}dq\] And to find total work \[W\] in charging the conductor to charge \[Q\] is, \[\int{dW=\int\limits_{0}^{Q}{\frac{q}{C}dq}}\,\] \[\Rightarrow W=\frac{{{Q}^{2}}}{2C}\] Thus energy stored in a conductor, having capacitance C and charge Q with final potential V is: \[U=\frac{{{Q}^{2}}}{2C}=\frac{1}{2}C{{V}^{2}}=\frac{QV}{2}\] [Using Q = CV] This self-energy is stored in the region of electric field due to the conductor.

\(\qquad W = \int_0^Q V \, dQ = \int_0^Q \frac{Q}{C} \, dQ = \frac{Q^2}{2C} \)

Energy Stored in Terms of Capacitance

The energy stored in a charged conductor is:

\(\qquad U = \frac{1}{2} C V^2 \)

This formula highlights that the energy depends on both the capacitance and the square of the potential.

Connecting Isolated Conductors

When two or more isolated conductors are connected by a conducting wire, they share charge until they reach the same potential. This redistribution depends on the initial charge and capacitance of the conductors.

Charge Redistribution Between Two Conductors

Consider two isolated conductors with capacitances \( C_1 \) and \( C_2 \), initially charged to potentials \( V_1 \) and \( V_2 \), respectively. Upon connection, charges redistribute, and the system reaches a common potential \( V_f \). Conservation of charge and the principle of equipotentiality lead to:

\(\qquad Q_1 + Q_2 = C_1 V_f + C_2 V_f \)

\(\qquad V_f = \frac{C_1 V_1 + C_2 V_2}{C_1 + C_2} \)

Charge Redistribution Among Multiple Conductors

For \( n \) conductors, the charge redistribution follows a similar principle. The total charge in the system remains constant, and the final potential \( V_f \) can be calculated using:

\(\qquad V_f = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n C_i V_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n C_i} \)

Heat Generated During Redistribution

When two conductors are connected, the redistribution of charge can result in the generation of heat due to resistive losses. The heat \( H \) generated is the difference between the initial energy of the system and the final energy:

\(\qquad H = \frac{1}{2} C_1 V_1^2 + \frac{1}{2} C_2 V_2^2 - \frac{1}{2} (C_1 + C_2) V_f^2 \)

Substituting \( V_f \) from the earlier formula ensures conservation of energy in the system.

Capacitors come in various shapes and configurations, each designed for specific applications. Here are some common types:

Ideal Design Considerations

An ideal capacitor should:

  • Have infinite dielectric strength (to prevent breakdown)
  • Have zero resistance in the conducting plates
  • Store maximum energy for minimum size
  • Have minimal energy loss (leakage)
Parallel Plate Capacitor

The most basic type consists of two parallel conducting plates separated by a distance \(d\).

Charge Distribution

Charges \(+Q\) and \(-Q\) reside on the facing surfaces of the plates, creating a uniform charge density \(\sigma = \frac{Q}{A}\), where \(A\) is the plate area.

DIAGRAM HERE: Parallel plate capacitor with charges and charge density indicated.
Electric Field

A uniform electric field exists between the plates, directed from the positive to the negative plate, with magnitude:

\(E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0 A}\)

DIAGRAM HERE: E field lines between the plates.
Force Between Plates

The plates attract each other due to the electric field. The force on each plate is:

\(F = \frac{Q^2}{2\epsilon_0 A}\)

Potential Difference

The potential difference \(V\) between the plates is:

\(V = Ed = \frac{Qd}{\epsilon_0 A}\)

Capacitance

The capacitance of a parallel plate capacitor is:

\(C = \frac{Q}{V} = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}\)

Energy Stored

The energy stored in a charged parallel plate capacitor is:

\(U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}QV\)

Work done to charge the capacitor equals the stored energy.

Electrostatic Potential Energy Density

Energy density \(u\) is the energy stored per unit volume.
