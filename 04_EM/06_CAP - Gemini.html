Definition of capacitance of isolated conductors

When a charge \(Q\) is given to a conductor, its potential increases.

[Diagram of a charged conductor with increased potential]

It has been experimentally observed that for an isolated conductor, there is always a fixed ratio between its charge \(Q\) and potential \(V\) at its surface.

\( Q \propto V\)

\( \Rightarrow \quad Q = C V\)

This proportionality constant is defined as the capacitance \(C\). It depends solely on the geometric structure of the conductor and is independent of the charge and potential.

In SI unit, capacitance is defined as \(\text{Farad} \quad (F)\). \(1 \text{ Farad}\) is capacitance of a conductor when \(1 \text{ Coulomb}\) charge raises its potential to \(1 \text{ Volt}\).

The concept of capacitance can be best explained with an analogy: consider two rooms where people enter one after another. Room 1 has less space; consequently, even with a smaller number of people inside, the room gets crowded and its discomfort level (analogous to potential) increases rapidly. In contrast, room 2 offers more space, requiring a larger number of people to achieve the same discomfort level as room 1. The space required or size of a room here defines its capacitance in electrical systems, determining how much charge the conductor can hold at a specific potential level.

[gif/glb for comparison: showing crowdedness and discomfort level on adding people sequentially into two different size of room/box]

Factors affecting the capacitance

The following factors influence the capacitance of an isolated conductor:

  • Size and shape of the conductor: A larger conductor can hold more charge at a given potential. This is analogous to how a larger container holds more water before overflowing.
  • Surrounding medium: Placing conductors near each other enhances their capacitance. In contrast, if an insulated conducting spherical shell covers a spherical charged conductor, the external shell lowers the central conductor's potential. Thus placing grounded/conducting cover near isolated charged conductors, reduces their potential, hence increase capacitance
Capacitance of spherical conductor

Consider an isolated spherical conductor of radius \(R\), located in free space. When a charge \(Q\) is given to the spherical conductor, its potential is:

\(\qquad \displaystyle V=\dfrac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0} \dfrac{Q}{R}\)

Therefore capacitance,

\( C=\dfrac{Q}{V} \)

\(\Rightarrow\quad \displaystyle C=\dfrac{Q}{Q/(4\pi \epsilon_0 R)}=4\pi \epsilon_0 R\)

So, we find:

\( \displaystyle C=4\pi\epsilon_0R\)

Charge redistribution for two conductors

If two conductors have initial charges \(Q_1 \text{ and }Q_2\), when they are connected by conducting wire they redistribute charges till equilibrium. This equilibrium is attained when the charges present on both are constant with time.

Since a steady state has no time-varying charges or fields, the electrostatic potential inside conductors must be the same, resulting in identical potentials of connected conductors at all their points after charge redistribution. After connections, they obtain common potential \(V\). So the circuit diagram will become like the one below:

[diagram showing two connected conductors after charge redistribution with common potential.]

Charge \(q_1 \) flows from conductor \(1\) and \(q_2\) flows into conductor \(2\) to get same potential \(V\). Also

\(\qquad q_1 + (-q_2) = 0 \)

Hence charge is always conserved when redistributed, no matter the number of isolated conductors.

If initial total charge is \(q=Q_1+Q_2\) (when they were at their individual potential) then after connecting them with conducting wires:

  • \(\qquad Q'_1+Q'_2=Q_1+Q_2=q \)[conservation of charge.]
  • \(\qquad \dfrac{Q_1'}{C_1}=\dfrac{Q_2'}{C_2} \)[same potential of two conductors after connection.]

Solving for \(Q'_1 \text{ and }Q'_2\) in the two equations we get:

\(\qquad Q'_1=q \left(\dfrac{C_1}{C_1+C_2}\right)\)

\(\qquad Q'_2=q \left(\dfrac{C_2}{C_1+C_2}\right)\)

Charge redistribution for multiple conductors

If isolated conductors \(C_1,\ C_2,...C_n \) are at \(V_1, \ V_2, ...V_n \) potential having charges \(q_1, q_2,..., q_n \) are connected via conducting wire simultaneously, charges start flowing within them such that:

  • Total charge always remains the same at any instant. Hence \( q_{Total}\) will be a constant. If charges after the redistribution are:

    \( q'_1,q'_2,\ldots q'_n \), then:

    \(\qquad \sum_1^n{q_i} =\sum_1^n {q'_i}=q_\text{Total}\)
  • Also the total system tries to come to a state where electrostatic fields remain zero inside the conducting material (this will correspond to constant time varying potential on conductor and thus electric fields vanish on its interior.)
  • It can only occur when the potentials of the isolated conducting regions will equal to some fixed \(V\) when in steady state.

Now suppose total capacitance (after connecting all capacitance through conducting wire):

\(\qquad\displaystyle C_{\text{Total}}= \sum_{i=1}^n{C_i} \)

So \(Q_\text{total}\) can be rewritten in the following two equivalent forms. The circuit diagram in both cases remains the same after equilibrium attained:

  • \(\qquad Q_\text{Total}=q=\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^n q_i=\sum_{i=1}^n C_i v_i \)[when the conductors are at their initial charges]
  • \(\qquad Q_\text{Total}=\displaystyle V C_\text{Total}=V \sum_{i=1}^n C_i \)[when equilibrium reached on connecting with conducting wire.]

Therefore:

\(\qquad \displaystyle V=\dfrac{q}{\sum_{i=1}^n C_i}=\frac{\sum_1^n C_iv_i}{\sum_1^n C_i} \)

[Diagram after charges redistributed between n conductors in steady state connected to conducting wire (name variables on them)]

Hence charges on them:

\(\qquad Q'_i=C_i V \)

\(\qquad\displaystyle\Rightarrow\quad Q'_i= q \left(\frac{C_i}{\sum_{j=1}^n C_j}\right)\)

Energy stored in isolated conductors

Charging isolated conductors, akin to accumulating electrons (hence energy) onto the surface. Each infinitesimal charge element \( dq \) incrementally builds potential by \( dV=\dfrac{dq}{C} \). Therefore, energy required to bring this small charge from infinite to near isolated conductors at a certain potential is analogous to adding an element of energy which gets stored there. \(\qquad \int_0^Q \dfrac{q}{C} \,dq \)

Work done in charging an isolated conductor

In total energy calculation, when infinitesimally small charges brought to a certain isolated conductor sequentially.

\( \displaystyle\int_0^Q {V\,dq}=\int_0^Q{\frac{q}{C}\,dq}=\dfrac{Q^2}{2C}=\frac{1}{2}CV^2= \frac{1}{2}QV \)

Energy stored in terms of capacitance

This stored energy \(U\) on an isolated charged conductor of capacitance \( C \) can be represented as:

\(\qquad \displaystyle U=\dfrac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2} CV^2 = \frac{1}{2} QV\)

Ideal design of capacitors

Capacitors are electronic components designed specifically to store electrical charge. An ideal capacitor would retain a charge for an infinite amount of time without loss and also be capable of charging and discharging extremely quickly, much faster than other components in the circuit.

A standard capacitor, consists of two large conducting plates closely spaced together with a relatively small separation, and they can come in either circular or rectangular forms.

Electric Field between the plates

To derive a formula for the electric field in a parallel-plate capacitor with plate charge densities of \(+\sigma\) and \(-\sigma\), the concept of flux can be applied to an infinitely large charged sheet:

\(\qquad \displaystyle E= \dfrac{\sigma}{2 \epsilon_0}\)

In a parallel-plate configuration, the superposition principle allows us to derive an expression for the field between the plates, recognizing that one plate shields another as explained in diagram

This expression \( \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} \) accurately calculates the total field \( E \) between the two parallel plates of opposite polarities.

Relation of self and interaction energy

For a system of charged particles, the total electrostatic potential energy can be decomposed into two parts:

  • Self-energy: This is the energy associated with assembling each individual charge distribution, assuming other charges are absent.
  • Interaction energy: This is the energy due to the interactions between the charge distributions.

Mathematically, for a system with two charge distributions:

\(\qquad U_{\text{total}} = U_{\text{self,1}} + U_{\text{self,2}} + U_{\text{interaction}} \)

For Example: If a system consists of two charged conductors with charges \( q_1\) and \(q_2\), the interaction energy \(U_{int}\) between them is defined as:

\(\qquad U_{int}= \frac{1}{2}(q_1 V_{21} + q_2 V_{12})\)

where \(V_{21} \text{ and } V_{12}\) is the potential on the charges present on the \(1^{st}\) conductor due to charge present on the second and potential at the charges present on the \(2^{nd}\) conductor due to first. Because Newton's third law also works in electrostatics too. \(V_{21} \propto \) charge present on second\(= q_2 \) and \( V_{12} \propto \) charge on first \(= q_1 \)

Hence \(q_1 V_{21} = q_2 V_{12}\)

So \(\qquad \displaystyle U_\text{int} = q_1 V_{21} =q_2 V_{12}\)
