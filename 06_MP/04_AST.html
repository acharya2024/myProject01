Atomic Structure

All About Hydrogen Like Systems

The Rutherford model of the atom was proposed by Ernest Rutherford in 1911, based on his famous gold foil experiment. This model describes the atom as having a small, dense, positively charged nucleus at its center, with electrons orbiting around it.

To investigate the structure of the atom, Rutherford and his colleagues directed a beam of alpha particles at a very thin sheet of gold foil. Alpha particles are helium nuclei consisting of two protons and two neutrons, symbolized as \( \alpha \) particles. They are positively charged and possess significant kinetic energy, making them suitable probes for exploring atomic structure.

Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment

In the experimental setup, a radioactive source emitted alpha particles, which were collimated into a narrow beam and directed at the gold foil target. A fluorescent screen or detector surrounded the foil to observe the scattering of alpha particles. This arrangement is depicted in the diagram below.

As the alpha particles passed through the gold foil, most of them experienced little to no deflection, indicating that atoms are mostly empty space. However, a small fraction of alpha particles were deflected at large angles, and some even bounced back toward the source. This surprising result suggested that a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus resides at the center of the atom.

Nuclear Size Estimation

To quantify the size of the nucleus, Rutherford calculated the distance of closest approach (\( r_0 \)) of an alpha particle during a head-on collision with the nucleus. For this complete conversion of kinetic energy into potential energy to occur, the collision must be perfectly head-on.

At the point of closest approach, all kinetic energy is converted to potential energy:

\(\qquad \dfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \dfrac{Z e \cdot 2e}{r_0} \)

Solving for \( r_0 \), we obtain:

\(\qquad r_0 = \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \dfrac{2 Z e^2}{\dfrac{1}{2} m v^2} \)

Where:

  • \( m \) is the mass of the alpha particle.
  • \( v \) is the initial velocity of the alpha particle.
  • \( Z \) is the atomic number of the gold nucleus.
  • \( e \) is the elementary charge.
  • \( \varepsilon_0 \) is the vacuum permittivity.

Additionally, Rutherford derived a formula for the fraction of alpha particles deflected by an angle \( \theta \):

\(\qquad \dfrac{dN}{d\Omega} = \left( \dfrac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0} \cdot \dfrac{Z e^2}{2 m v^2} \right)^2 \cdot \dfrac{1}{\sin^4 \left( \dfrac{\theta}{2} \right)} \)

In this formula:

  • \( dN \) is the number of particles scattered into a solid angle \( d\Omega \).
  • \( \theta \) is the scattering angle.
  • \( Z \) is the atomic number of the target nucleus.
  • \( e \) is the elementary charge.
  • \( m \) is the mass of the alpha particle.
  • \( v \) is the velocity of the alpha particle.
  • \( \varepsilon_0 \) is the vacuum permittivity.

This formula shows that the number of particles scattered at a particular angle depends on the atomic number \( Z \), the kinetic energy of the alpha particles, and the thickness of the foil. The expected scattering pattern, as illustrated in the accompanying diagram, demonstrates the distribution of deflected alpha particles, providing further evidence for the nuclear model.

These calculations indicated that the nucleus is extremely small compared to the size of the atom, occupying a tiny volume where most of the mass and positive charge are concentrated.

Drawbacks of Rutherford's Model

Despite its revolutionary insights, Rutherford's model had significant limitations:

  • It could not explain the discrete spectral lines observed in hydrogen's emission spectrum.
  • Classical electromagnetism predicts that accelerating charges, like electrons in orbit, should emit radiation and lose energy, causing them to spiral into the nucleus, which does not occur.

According to classical physics, why is Rutherford's atomic model considered unstable? Orbiting electrons should emit radiation and spiral into the nucleus The positively charged nucleus would repel the electrons, causing the atom to disintegrate

Why did Rutherford's observations contradict the then-accepted plum pudding model of the atom? Because large-angle deflections suggested a concentrated positive charge, not a diffuse one Because the plum pudding model predicted all alpha particles would be absorbed

To address these shortcomings, Niels Bohr proposed a new atomic model incorporating quantum concepts.

Niels Bohr introduced a new atomic model in 1913 to overcome the limitations of Rutherford's model. Bohr's model incorporates quantum theory to explain the stability of atoms and the discrete spectral lines of hydrogen.

    Bohr proposed the following postulates:

  • Stationary Orbits:

    Electrons revolve around the nucleus in certain stable orbits without emitting radiation. These orbits are called stationary states.

  • Quantization of Angular Momentum:

    The angular momentum of an electron in a stationary orbit is quantized and given by:

    \(\qquad L = m v r = n \dfrac{h}{2\pi} \)

    where \( n \) is a positive integer (principal quantum number), and \( h \) is Planck's constant.

  • Emission and Absorption of Photons:

    Electrons can transition between stationary orbits by emitting or absorbing a photon with energy equal to the difference between the initial and final energy levels:

    \(\qquad E_{\text{photon}} = E_i - E_f = h \nu \)

According to Bohr's first postulate, why don't electrons emit radiation while orbiting the nucleus in certain orbits? Because they occupy stationary states where they do not lose energy Because they are at rest and not moving around the nucleus

In Bohr's model, can an electron have any arbitrary value of angular momentum? Yes, depending on its speed and orbit radius No, it can only have specific quantized values

How do electrons emit or absorb energy in Bohr's atomic model? By jumping between quantized energy levels and emitting or absorbing photons By continuously radiating energy as they orbit the nucleus

How does Bohr's model resolve the issue of atomic instability present in Rutherford's model? By proposing that electrons occupy stable, quantized orbits without radiating energy By suggesting that electrons do not move and are fixed in place around the nucleus

Bohr's quantization condition for angular momentum can be explained using the concept of standing waves. The circumference of the electron's orbit must be an integral multiple of the electron's de Broglie wavelength:

\(\qquad 2\pi r = n \lambda \)

This shows the equivalence between the wave nature of electrons and Bohr's quantization of angular momentum.

  • Charge in Magnetic Field

    Consider a charged particle \( q \) moving in a circular path of radius \( r \) perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field \( B \). The magnetic force provides the necessary centripetal force:

    \(\qquad q v B = \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \)

    Applying Bohr's quantization condition:

    \(\qquad m v r = n \dfrac{h}{2\pi} \)

    Combining the two equations to solve for the possible radii \( r \):

    From the magnetic force equation: \( \qquad \quad r = \dfrac{m v}{q B} \)

    Substituting \( v \) from Bohr's condition:

    \(\qquad r = \dfrac{m \left( \dfrac{n h}{2\pi m r} \right)}{q B} = \dfrac{n h}{2\pi q B r} \)

    \(\Rightarrow\quad r^2 = \dfrac{n h}{2\pi q B} \)

    \(\Rightarrow \quad r = \sqrt{ \dfrac{n h}{2\pi q B} } \)

    Therefore, the possible radii \( r \) are given by: \(\qquad r_n = \sqrt{ \dfrac{n h}{2\pi q B} } \)

    These results illustrate how Bohr's quantization condition leads to only discrete possible radii for a charged particle in a magnetic field.

  • Particle in a General Potential Field

    Consider a particle of mass \( m \) moving under a potential energy \( U = \dfrac{1}{2} m \omega^2 r^2 \). The force acting on the particle is:

    \(\qquad F = -\dfrac{dU}{dr} = -m \omega^2 r \)

    The negative sign indicates that the force is attractive towards the origin. Setting the centripetal force equal to the attractive force:

    \(\qquad \dfrac{m v^2}{r} = m \omega^2 r \)

    Simplifying: \(\qquad v = \omega r \)

    Applying Bohr's quantization condition: \(\qquad m v r = n \dfrac{h}{2\pi} \)

    Substituting \( v = \omega r \) into the quantization condition:

    \(\qquad m \omega r^2 = n \dfrac{h}{2\pi} \)

    \( \Rightarrow \quad r^2 = \dfrac{n h}{2\pi m \omega} \)

    \(\Rightarrow\quad r_n = \sqrt{ \dfrac{n h}{2\pi m \omega} } \)

    These results demonstrate how Bohr's quantization condition leads to discrete radii for a particle in a harmonic potential field.

Effect of Finite Mass of Nucleus

In Bohr's original model of the hydrogen atom, the nucleus is considered infinitely massive compared to the electron. This simplification allows the nucleus to remain stationary while the electron orbits around it. However, in reality, the nucleus has a finite mass and cannot be treated as an immovable point. Both the nucleus and the electron experience mutual electromagnetic attractions, causing them to accelerate towards each other. Consequently, they revolve around a common center of mass (barycenter), which remains stationary in the absence of external forces.

To account for the finite mass of the nucleus, we introduce the reduced mass (\( \mu \)), which replaces the electron mass \( m \) in Bohr's quantization condition:

\(\qquad \mu = \dfrac{m M}{m + M}\)

Here, \( M \) is the mass of the nucleus. This correction adjusts the calculated radius and energy levels of the electron's orbit.

Center of Mass Motion

The center of mass (\( R \)) of the electron-nucleus system is defined by:

\(\qquad R = \frac{m r_e + M r_n}{m + M}\)

where:

  • \( m \) is the mass of the electron,
  • \( M \) is the mass of the nucleus,
  • \( r_e \) is the position vector of the electron,
  • \( r_n \) is the position vector of the nucleus.

In the absence of external forces, the center of mass remains at rest or moves with a constant velocity. This means both the electron and the nucleus orbit this fixed point, with their velocities and radii inversely proportional to their masses.

Introduction of Reduced Mass (\( \mu \))

To incorporate the finite mass of the nucleus into Bohr's model, we use the reduced mass \( \mu \), allowing us to treat the two-body problem as an effective single-body problem. The reduced mass is given by:

\(\qquad \mu = \frac{m M}{m + M}\)

Since \( M \) is typically much larger than \( m \) (for hydrogen, \( M \approx 1836 \, m \)), the reduced mass \( \mu \) is slightly less than the electron mass \( m \). This small reduction significantly impacts the calculated radii and energy levels.

Why does the finite mass of the nucleus require the introduction of reduced mass in Bohr's model? Because both particles orbit a common center, altering the dynamics Because the nucleus exerts no force on the electron when it has finite mass

Does the reduced mass \( \mu \) become greater than the electron mass \( m \) when considering the finite nuclear mass? Yes, \( \mu > m \) No, \( \mu < m \)

Impact on Radius and Energy Levels

1. Radius (\( r_n \)):

The radius of the \( n \)-th orbit in Bohr's model is originally given by:

\(\qquad r_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{4 \pi^2 m e^2}\)

Incorporating the reduced mass:

\(\qquad r_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{4 \pi^2 \mu e^2}\)

Since \( \mu < m \), the corrected radius \( r_n \) becomes slightly larger than the radius calculated assuming an infinite nuclear mass.

2. Energy Levels (\( E_n \)):

The energy of the electron in the \( n \)-th orbit is initially:

\(\qquad E_n = -\frac{m e^4}{8 \epsilon_0^2 h^2 n^2}\)

With the introduction of reduced mass:

\(\qquad E_n = -\frac{\mu e^4}{8 \epsilon_0^2 h^2 n^2}\)

This correction results in a slightly more negative (lower) energy level, indicating a more tightly bound electron.

Numerical Example for Hydrogen Atom

For the hydrogen atom (\( M \approx 1836 \, m \)), the reduced mass is approximately:

\(\qquad \mu \approx \frac{m \times 1836 m}{m + 1836 m} = \frac{1836 m^2}{1837 m} \approx 0.99946 \, m\)

This shows that the reduced mass is only marginally less than the electron mass, resulting in very slight adjustments to the calculated radii and energy levels. For heavier elements where \( M \) is significantly larger, the effect of the reduced mass becomes even less pronounced.

Drawbacks of Bohr's Model

Despite its success, Bohr's model has limitations:

  • It cannot explain spectra of atoms with more than one electron.
  • It does not account for the fine structure and splitting of spectral lines.
  • It violates the principles of uncertainty and does not incorporate wave-particle duality.
  • It cannot explain the Zeeman effect (splitting of spectral lines in magnetic fields).

In an atom, electrons occupy specific energy levels or orbits as defined by Bohr's postulates. The lowest energy level is called the ground state, while higher energy levels are excited states. When an electron transitions between energy levels, a photon is either emitted or absorbed. If the electron moves to a higher energy level, it absorbs a photon (excitation). If it drops to a lower energy level, it emits a photon (de-excitation).

During these processes, the energy change \( \Delta E \) equals the energy of the photon:

\(\qquad \Delta E = E_{\text{final}} - E_{\text{initial}} = h \nu \)

In atomic transitions, only one photon is emitted or absorbed per transition. This ensures that each energy level change corresponds to a distinct photon emission or absorption event. The process of transition is extremely rapid, occurring on the order of \( 10^{-8} \) seconds.

Consider a sample of single-electron systems where all electrons are initially in an excited state denoted by \(n_i\). During the process of de-excitation, it is not necessary for every electron to transition directly to the ground state in a single step. Instead, electrons can move through a series of intermediate energy levels before reaching the ground state.

For each energy level above the ground state, there are multiple lower energy levels to which an electron can transition. Specifically, an electron in the \(n\)-th energy level has the possibility to move down to any of the \(n - 1\) different lower energy levels. For instance, an electron in the 4th energy level (\(n = 4\)) can transition to the 3rd, 2nd, or 1st energy levels.

Moreover, once an electron transitions to the \((n - 1)\)-th energy level, it can further transition down to \(n - 2\) different lower energy levels, and so on, until it finally reaches the ground state. This step-by-step transition process allows for multiple pathways by which an electron can de-excite.

The total number of unique transitions (and consequently, the number of spectral lines observed) can be calculated by summing the possible transitions from each energy level. This is given by the sum of the first \(n - 1\) natural numbers:

\(\qquad N = \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} k = 1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + (n - 1) \)

\(\Rightarrow\quad N = \dfrac{n(n - 1)}{2} \)

For example, if an electron is in the 4th energy level (\(n = 4\)), the number of possible transitions is:

\(\qquad N = \dfrac{4 \times (4 - 1)}{2} = \dfrac{12}{2} = 6 \)

The spectral lines of hydrogen are grouped into distinct series based on the final energy level \( n_f \) to which the electron transitions. Each series corresponds to a specific range of wavelengths and lies within a particular region of the electromagnetic spectrum. Below is a detailed overview of each series, including calculations for minimum and maximum wavelengths and the spectral regions they inhabit.

  • Lyman Series: \( n_f = 1 \) (Ultraviolet Region)

    The Lyman series involves transitions where electrons fall to the first energy level (\( n_f = 1 \)) from higher energy levels (\( n_i \geq 2 \)). This series lies entirely in the ultraviolet (UV) region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

    Wavelength Calculations:

    The wavelengths of the spectral lines in the Lyman series can be calculated using the Rydberg formula:

    \(\qquad \frac{1}{\lambda} = R \left( 1 - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right) \) Where:

    • \( \lambda \) is the wavelength of the emitted photon.
    • \( R \) is the Rydberg constant (\(1.097 \times 10^7 \, \text{m}^{-1}\)).
    • \( n_i \) is the initial energy level (\( n_i \ge 2 \)).

    Minimum Wavelength (\( \lambda_{\text{min}} \)) occurs when \( n_i \) approaches infinity:

    \(\qquad \lambda_{\text{min}} = \frac{1}{R \left( \frac{1}{1^2} - 0 \right)} = \frac{1}{R} \approx 91.2 \, \text{nm} \)

    Maximum Wavelength (\( \lambda_{\text{max}} \)) occurs when \( n_i = 2 \):

    \(\qquad \lambda_{\text{max}} = \frac{1}{R \left( 1 - \frac{1}{2^2} \right)} = \frac{1}{R \times \frac{3}{4}} \approx 121.6 \, \text{nm} \)

    The Lyman series transitions produce photons in the ultraviolet region, making them invisible to the naked eye.

  • Balmer Series: \( n_f = 2 \) (Visible Region)

    The Balmer series involves transitions where electrons fall to the second energy level (\( n_f = 2 \)) from higher energy levels (\( n_i \geq 3 \)). This series is significant because it includes visible spectral lines, which are observable with the naked eye.

    Wavelength Calculations:

    Using the Rydberg formula:

    \(\qquad \frac{1}{\lambda} = R \left( \frac{1}{2^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right) \) Where \( n_i \geq 3 \).

    Minimum Wavelength (\( \lambda_{\text{min}} \)) occurs when \( n_i \) approaches infinity:

    \(\qquad \lambda_{\text{min}} = \frac{1}{R \left( \frac{1}{4} - 0 \right)} = \frac{4}{R} \approx 364.5 \, \text{nm} \)

    Maximum Wavelength (\( \lambda_{\text{max}} \)) occurs when \( n_i = 3 \):

    \(\qquad \lambda_{\text{max}} = \frac{1}{R \left( \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{9} \right)} = \frac{36}{5 R} \approx 656.3 \, \text{nm} \)

    The Balmer series transitions produce visible light, with the most prominent lines being:

    • H-alpha: \( n_i = 3 \), \( \lambda = 656.3 \, \text{nm} \) (Red)
    • H-beta: \( n_i = 4 \), \( \lambda = 486.1 \, \text{nm} \) (Blue-Green)
    • H-gamma: \( n_i = 5 \), \( \lambda = 434.0 \, \text{nm} \) (Blue)
    • H-delta: \( n_i = 6 \), \( \lambda = 410.2 \, \text{nm} \) (Violet)

    These lines form the visible spectrum of hydrogen and are commonly observed in hydrogen emission spectra.

  • Paschen Series: \( n_f = 3 \) (Infrared Region)

    The Paschen series involves transitions where electrons fall to the third energy level (\( n_f = 3 \)) from higher energy levels (\( n_i \geq 4 \)). This series lies in the infrared (IR) region of the electromagnetic spectrum.

    Wavelength Calculations:

    Using the Rydberg formula:

    \(\qquad \frac{1}{\lambda} = R \left( \frac{1}{3^2} - \frac{1}{n_i^2} \right) \) Where \( n_i \geq 4 \).

    Minimum Wavelength (\( \lambda_{\text{min}} \)) occurs when \( n_i \) approaches infinity:

    \(\qquad \lambda_{\text{min}} = \frac{1}{R \left( \frac{1}{9} - 0 \right)} = \frac{9}{R} \approx 820.7 \, \text{nm} \)

    Maximum Wavelength (\( \lambda_{\text{max}} \)) occurs when \( n_i = 4 \):

    \(\qquad \lambda_{\text{max}} = \frac{1}{R \left( \frac{1}{9} - \frac{1}{16} \right)} = \frac{144}{7 R} \approx 1875.1 \, \text{nm} \)

    The Paschen series transitions emit infrared radiation, which is not visible to the naked eye but can be detected using specialized instruments. These lines are important in various applications, including astronomy and infrared spectroscopy.

Recoiling of Atoms During Transition

Conservation of momentum dictates that when a photon is emitted, the atom recoils in the opposite direction. The momentum of the photon is \( p_{\text{photon}} = \dfrac{E}{c} \), and by conservation of momentum, the atom gains an equal and opposite momentum \( p_{\text{atom}} = -\dfrac{E}{c} \).

The kinetic energy of the recoiling atom is:

\(\qquad K_{\text{atom}} = \dfrac{p_{\text{atom}}^2}{2 M} = \dfrac{(E/c)^2}{2 M} \)

Since the mass \( M \) of the atom is much larger than the energy \( E \) divided by the speed of light \( c \), the recoil energy \( K_{\text{atom}} \) is negligible compared to the energy of the emitted photon \( E \). Therefore, the recoil of the atom does not significantly affect the energy calculations of the emitted photon.

Transition in Moving Atoms

For atoms moving at significant velocities, Doppler effect and relativistic corrections may need to be considered when analyzing spectral lines and transition energies. The observed frequency \( \nu' \) is shifted due to the motion of the atom:

\(\qquad \nu' = \nu \left( 1 \pm \dfrac{v}{c} \right) \)

Where \( v \) is the velocity of the atom along the line of sight, and the positive sign corresponds to the atom moving away from the observer.

At the atomic scale, it is not appropriate to consider energy losses in the form of friction, heating, sound, or deformation of the body, as we do with macroscopic objects. For instance, heating is still a form of kinetic energy at the atomic level, and deformation involves the realignment of arrays of molecules, which does not apply to individual atoms. Instead, energy loss in atomic collisions occurs through quantum mechanical processes such as electronic excitations or ionizations. These quantum transitions are fundamentally different from the classical dissipative mechanisms observed in larger-scale interactions.

In atomic collisions, energy exchange determines the type of collision that occurs. An inelastic collision happens when kinetic energy is not conserved due to internal energy changes, such as the excitation or ionization of electrons. For a collision to be inelastic, the kinetic energy (\( K \)) of the incident particle must be sufficient to cause a transition in the target atom.

Specifically, the energy loss (\( \Delta E \)) during the collision must be equal to or greater than the energy difference between the electron's initial and final states. If this condition is met (\( K \geq \Delta E \)), the collision results in the excitation or ionization of the electron, and kinetic energy may not be conserved. Otherwise, the collision is elastic, and kinetic energy remains conserved without any internal energy changes.

Illustrative Example: Head-on Collision of Neutron with Hydrogen

Consider a neutron colliding head-on with a hydrogen atom in its ground state at rest. The nature of the collisionwhether it is elastic or inelasticdepends on the kinetic energy (\( K \)) of the neutron relative to the energy required to excite the electron in the hydrogen atom.

The energy levels of a hydrogen atom are quantized, with specific energy differences between levels. The possible energy transitions (\( \Delta E \)) are:

\(\qquad \Delta E = \{0, 10.2\, \text{eV}, 12.09\, \text{eV}, \dots, 13.6\, \text{eV}\} \)

According to classical mechanics, the minimum energy loss in a collision is zero (elastic collision), while the maximum energy loss occurs in a perfectly inelastic collision. In a perfectly inelastic head-on collision between the neutron and the hydrogen atom, momentum conservation dictates:

\(\qquad m v_0 = (m + m) v_f \)

Assuming the mass of the neutron is approximately equal to the mass of the hydrogen atom (\( m \)), we have:

\(\qquad m v_0 = 2 m v_f \implies v_f = \frac{v_0}{2} \)

The final kinetic energy (\( K_f \)) of the system is then:

\(\qquad K_f = \frac{1}{2} (m + m) v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2} (2 m) \left( \frac{v_0}{2} \right)^2 \)

\(\Rightarrow\quad K_f = \frac{1}{4} m v_0^2 \)

\(\Rightarrow\quad K_f = \frac{K}{2} \)

Therefore, the maximum possible energy loss (\( \Delta E_{\text{max}} \)) in the collision is \(\dfrac{K}{2}\):

To determine whether the collision is elastic or inelastic for different kinetic energies, we compare \( \Delta E_{\text{max}} \) with the energy required for excitation (\( \Delta E \)):

  • When \( K = 14\, \text{eV} \):

    The maximum energy loss is \( \frac{14\, \text{eV}}{2} = 7\, \text{eV} \). This is less than the minimum excitation energy (\( 10.2\, \text{eV} \)), so the collision cannot excite the hydrogen atom. Therefore, the collision is bound to be elastic.

  • When \( K = 20.4\, \text{eV} \):

    The maximum energy loss is \( \frac{20.4\, \text{eV}}{2} = 10.2\, \text{eV} \). This equals the minimum excitation energy, allowing the collision to be perfectly inelastic, resulting in the excitation of the hydrogen atom. So in this case, the collision can be either elastic or perfectly inelastic.

  • When \( K = 22\, \text{eV} \):

    The maximum energy loss is \( \frac{22\, \text{eV}}{2} = 11\, \text{eV} \). This exceeds the minimum excitation energy, so the collision can be inelastic, with a possible loss of \( 10.2\, \text{eV} \) of kinetic energy. Thus, possible types of collision in this case are elastic and inelastic collisions.

  • When \( K = 24.18\, \text{eV} \):

    The maximum energy loss is \( \frac{24.18\, \text{eV}}{2} = 12.09\, \text{eV} \). This allows for higher energy excitations, and the collision may be elastic (with no loss in kinetic energy), inelastic (with a loss of \( 10.2\, \text{eV} \)), or perfectly inelastic (with a loss of \( 12.09\, \text{eV} \)).

Understanding the conditions under which collisions are elastic or inelastic is essential in fields like nuclear physics and material science, where atomic interactions influence the behavior of matter.
